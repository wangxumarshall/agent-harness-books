# Chapter 12: The Storms of the Future — Binance's Global Game and the Rise of New Forces

> *"The true king is not the one who never falls, but the one who learns to dance in the storm."*
> *— Changpeng Zhao (CZ)*

> *"The world of crypto was never a stagnant pond, but a river that rushes on forever. Old heroes fall, new ones rise — only the storms, they never cease."*

---

## Prologue: The Summer of 2017, the Crossroads of Fate

The summer of 2017, and the Shanghai skyline shimmered beneath the heat rising from data centers.

Deep beneath this Chinese financial hub, countless mining machines roared around the clock. Standing before the floor-to-ceiling windows of an office building in Pudong, forty-year-old Changpeng Zhao gazed out at the city. In the industry, everyone called him CZ.

He had just made a decision that would reshape the entire cryptocurrency world.

"Are you sure about this?" A voice came from behind him. It was He Yi — his comrade from the OKCoin days, one of the rare female leaders in the crypto space.

"We've discussed this countless times," Zhao's voice was calm but resolute. "The ICO market is exploding, but none of the exchanges can keep pace. OKCoin is too conservative, Huobi is too slow, Bitfinex has too many problems. The market needs a truly global, user-first, technology-driven platform."

"But the risks are enormous," He Yi walked into the conference room. "Regulatory storms are gathering. The People's Bank of China could strike at any moment, and the U.S. SEC is watching closely."

"It's like setting sail in the middle of a tempest," Zhao picked up the thread, a faint smile surfacing on his lips. "But precisely because there are storms, we need better captains."

He walked to the whiteboard and outlined his vision:

"First, we build a global exchange — not confined to any single country or region. Distributed servers, remote teams, an international legal structure.

Second, we deliver the ultimate user experience. A high-speed matching engine, multilingual support, 24/7 customer service.

Third, we issue our own token — BNB. This isn't just a fundraising vehicle; it's the core of the entire ecosystem. Use BNB to pay trading fees, use BNB to participate in IEOs, use BNB to build an entire DeFi ecosystem."

"How much will this cost?"

"We've raised fifteen million dollars," Zhao said. "That's enough to operate for a year. I plan to be a global top-ten exchange within three months, break into the top five in six months, and challenge for the top three within a year."

He Yi was silent for a moment. Then she extended her hand: "Alright, Captain — I'll sail with you."

Outside the window, thunder rumbled in from the distance. The summer of 2017, and a storm was brewing.

## Part One: Rising — A Lone Boat Sets Sail, Cutting Through the Waves

### 1. The Canadian Boy's Coding Dream

1977, Lianyungang, Jiangsu Province.

Changpeng Zhao was born into an intellectual family — his father a university professor, his mother a teacher. In 1989, twelve-year-old Changpeng Zhao emigrated with his parents to Vancouver, Canada.

"I remember the first day of school," CZ would later recall. "I couldn't understand a single word the teacher was saying. I sat in the back row of the classroom, feeling like an alien."

But the language barrier ignited a stubborn fire within him. Within six months, he had mastered English, and soon his academic talent began to shine through. Math was his strength — but what truly changed his life's trajectory was computers.

In 1993, Changpeng Zhao was sixteen. His father brought home a 486 computer — the most advanced personal computer of its era.

"I remember what that machine looked like," CZ later recalled. "It was a beige-colored tower, paired with a clunky CRT monitor. The moment I pressed the power button for the first time, I knew I had found my calling."

He threw himself into programming with a fervor bordering on obsession. BASIC, Pascal, C — one language after another fell under his command.

"Code possesses a pure beauty," he wrote in his journal. "Unlike human language, it is neither vague nor ambiguous. A line of code either works or it doesn't. No gray areas — only logic and creativity."

### 2. Tokyo and the Code-Driven Life

In 2001, Changpeng Zhao graduated from McGill University with a Bachelor of Science in Computer Science.

He chose the path of adventure — Tokyo. He joined a startup called Fusion Systems, building trading software for the Tokyo Stock Exchange. There, for the first time, he came face to face with the beating heart of the financial world.

"Finance is code," his boss told him. "In the electronic age, the market is one enormous computer. Whoever writes faster code wins in the market."

Zhao immersed himself in this world. He studied financial engineering, market microstructure, and how to write trading algorithms capable of making decisions at the microsecond level.

"Those years taught me three things," he later reflected. "First, speed is everything. Second, markets amplify emotions. Third, centralized systems are fragile."

In 2005, he moved to Bloomberg, tasked with developing futures trading software. At Bloomberg, Zhao learned how to build systems at scale and how to serve users across the globe.

He spent four years at Bloomberg, rising from programmer to technical director. In 2009, Bitcoin was born — but Zhao had not yet noticed this small project from the cryptography community.

### 3. Shanghai and the First Encounter with Bitcoin

In 2013, Zhao left his position at Bloomberg and returned to Asia in search of opportunity.

"I felt at the time that China would be the next center of tech entrepreneurship," he explained later. "There was an abundance of talent, a massive market, and unbelievable entrepreneurial energy."

In the autumn of 2013, on an afternoon like any other, he first heard of Bitcoin.

"A friend mentioned it over dinner," CZ recalled. "He said there was something called Bitcoin that had risen a hundredfold in a single year. My first reaction was: this must be a scam."

But as a technologist, he decided to investigate for himself. He downloaded Bitcoin's whitepaper and read it carefully under the lamplight of a late night.

"When I read about the blockchain concept, my mind was jolted like it had been struck by electricity," he said. "A decentralized ledger, proof of work, an immutable history — these concepts combined to create an entirely new mechanism for social coordination."

By the end of 2013, he had made the life-altering decision — to sell his Shanghai apartment and invest everything in Bitcoin.

"Real estate is tangible, but it is also rigid," he wrote in his journal. "Bitcoin is virtual, but it is fluid, global, and unstoppable."

He was partially right. Bitcoin's price crashed in the months that followed, plummeting from over $1,000 to under $200. His paper losses reached 80%. But he did not sell — he held on.

In 2014, he joined OKCoin, then China's leading Bitcoin exchange, as Chief Technology Officer.

### 4. The Battlefield of OKCoin

OKCoin, 2014. It was the golden age of Chinese Bitcoin exchanges.

Zhao's mandate was to rebuild the exchange's entire technical architecture. He led the team through a thorough technical overhaul — rewriting the matching engine, optimizing database structures, and introducing distributed systems design.

"CZ was a workaholic," a colleague recalled. "He could work consecutively for twenty hours in the office, sleep four hours on the sofa, and then get back to work."

During his time at OKCoin, Zhao mastered every detail of cryptocurrency exchange operations — how to partner with banks, how to navigate compliance, how to respond to hacker attacks.

But OKCoin was also a place steeped in controversy. Friction between founder Xu Mingxing's management style and Zhao's vision grew steadily sharper.

"We had different philosophies," CZ later put it diplomatically. "Mr. Xu wanted to build a China-focused exchange. I wanted to build a global platform."

In 2015, Zhao chose to leave OKCoin. He did not yet know that the timing would prove fortuitous two years down the road.

## Part Two: Ascending — An Empire Rises, the Winds of Fortune Converge

### 1. The Birth of Binance

June 2017, Malta.

Changpeng Zhao and his team were registering a new company — Binance. The name "Binance" was a portmanteau of "Binary" and "Finance."

"We want to build an exchange driven purely by code," he told the founding team. "No human interference, no political bias — only algorithms and mathematics."

But this vision faced enormous challenges. Fifteen million dollars in startup capital was barely a drop in the bucket for building a global cryptocurrency exchange.

"We must turn profitable quickly," Zhao told the team. "There is no time for slow burning. We need to break even within three months."

On July 14, 2017, Binance officially launched.

It was a quiet debut. No grand fanfare — just a simple tweet and a fully functional trading platform.

But within 24 hours of launch, registered users surpassed 10,000. A week later, that number became 100,000. Within a month, Binance had climbed into the top ten exchanges by trading volume worldwide.

"We caught the best possible wave," Zhao later admitted. "The 2017 ICO frenzy drove the entire market to madness. And we happened to fill exactly the gap that existed."

### 2. The Birth of BNB

Binance's success was not built on technology alone. One of Zhao's pivotal decisions was the launch of the exchange's own token — BNB (Binance Coin).

"BNB is far more than a fundraising tool," he wrote in the whitepaper. "It is the cornerstone of the Binance ecosystem. Holders of BNB receive discounts on trading fees and can participate in platform governance."

The total supply of BNB was set at 200 million tokens, with the ICO price equivalent to approximately $0.10 per BNB.

"We originally planned to raise fifteen million dollars," Zhao recalled. "In the end, we raised only about three million."

But BNB's design created a powerful network effect. Users holding BNB received trading fee discounts, incentivizing long-term holding.

"This is a flywheel," Zhao explained. "More users bring more trading volume. More trading volume brings more fee revenue. More revenue goes toward buybacks and burns of BNB."

### 3. The Turning Storm — China's Regulatory Hurricane

September 4, 2017, Beijing.

The People's Bank of China and six other ministries jointly issued the "Notice on Preventing Financial Risks from Initial Coin Offerings," declaring ICOs to be illegal public fundraising.

Bitcoin's price fell more than 20% within 24 hours. For Binance, this was a moment of existential crisis.

"Our office in China was shut down," Zhao recalled. "Our bank accounts were frozen. Overnight, we went from a legally operating company in China to an illegal entity."

But Zhao displayed the most valuable quality of an exceptional entrepreneur — calm amid chaos.

"We are moving," he told the team. "We are a global exchange. Our users are spread across the entire world."

Within days, Binance completed its exodus from China. Servers migrated overseas, the team dispersed to Japan, South Korea, Singapore, and beyond. For users, trading was virtually unaffected.

"The 9/4 incident forced us to accelerate globalization," Zhao said. "And in the end, that became our greatest advantage."

### 4. The Global Sprint

From late 2017 through early 2018, Binance entered a period of breakneck expansion.

In just a few months, Binance transformed from an upstart exchange into the world's largest cryptocurrency trading platform. Its 24-hour trading volume routinely exceeded three billion dollars, capturing over 20% of the global market.

"Our growth was exponential," Zhao recalled. "Every month, our user base doubled."

This growth brought enormous technical challenges. Zhao personally led multiple system upgrades — introducing microservices architecture, deploying a global CDN, and building automated scaling mechanisms.

"Technology is our most formidable moat," he said. "Replicating our technical architecture requires time and talent, and we were at least a year ahead of our competitors."

### 5. Malta's Dawn and the Regulatory Chess Game

March 2018, Malta.

This small Mediterranean island was becoming the "Blockchain Island" of the crypto world. Binance established its European headquarters in Malta, hiring hundreds of employees.

"Malta gave us a home," Zhao said. "Here, we didn't have to worry about being shut down overnight."

But even in Malta, regulatory pressure never fully subsided. In mid-2018, Japan's Financial Services Agency (FSA) issued Binance a warning, accusing it of serving Japanese users without a license.

"We were like someone running through a maze," Zhao described. "Every time you open one door, you find another wall."

To meet the regulatory challenge, Zhao began assembling a vast compliance team. He hired professionals from traditional finance and regulatory agencies.

"Compliance is not a cost — it is an investment," he said. "An exchange that cannot achieve compliance will never become a truly mainstream financial institution."

### 6. The Ecosystem Empire Expands

By 2019 and 2020, Binance was no longer content to be merely an exchange. In Zhao's vision, Binance would become a complete crypto ecosystem.

First came the launch of Binance Chain — Binance's own public blockchain, purpose-built for issuing and trading tokens.

"Ethereum is great, but it is too slow and too expensive," Zhao said. "We needed a chain optimized specifically for trading."

Next came the launch of Binance DEX — a decentralized exchange built atop Binance Chain.

"DEX is the direction of the future," Zhao declared. "Centralized exchanges will forever be targets for hackers. Decentralized exchanges eliminate single points of failure."

Then came Binance Launchpad — a token issuance platform through which projects could conduct IEOs (Initial Exchange Offerings).

"We want to be the gatekeepers of the industry," Zhao said. "Only rigorously vetted projects make it onto Binance Launchpad."

Beyond these, Binance launched a constellation of subsidiary brands: Binance Academy, Binance Research, Binance Charity, and the Binance Incubator, among others.

"Our goal is to make Binance the Google of the crypto world," Zhao said.

## Part Three: The Climax — Thunder Strikes, the Storm Descends

### 1. The Frenzied Bull Market of 2020

2020, as the COVID-19 pandemic swept across the globe. Traditional financial markets fell into panic — but the cryptocurrency world was writing a different story.

"Bitcoin was born in a financial crisis. It was made for moments like this," Zhao wrote on Twitter.

In the second half of 2020, institutional investors began entering the cryptocurrency market in force. Bitcoin's price climbed from $7,000 at the start of the year to $29,000 by year-end.

Binance became one of the biggest beneficiaries of this bull run.

"Our trading volume hit record highs," Zhao announced at an internal year-end meeting. "We processed over $100 billion in trades in a single day."

But prosperity bred new problems. System strain was first — during periods of violent price swings, Binance's platform suffered multiple outages. Then came human capital pressure, as the workforce ballooned from hundreds to thousands.

### 2. The Gathering Regulatory Storm

2021, and the world's regulatory bodies fundamentally shifted their stance on cryptocurrency.

China struck again, declaring all cryptocurrency trading illegal. The U.S. SEC launched investigations into multiple exchanges. The European Union, the United Kingdom, Japan, Singapore — nearly every major economy was tightening oversight.

"This is a turning point," Zhao said at an internal meeting. "The era of lawless growth is over."

Binance's distributed structure — once celebrated as a source of flexibility — had now become a compliance liability.

In May 2021, Bloomberg published a bombshell report exposing internal regulatory failures at Binance. The report alleged that Binance had long neglected anti-money laundering (AML) and Know Your Customer (KYC) requirements.

"These allegations are false," Zhao responded on Twitter. "We have invested hundreds of millions of dollars in compliance."

But the damage was done. Regulatory bodies in multiple countries opened investigations into Binance. The UK's FCA banned Binance from offering regulated services in the UK. Japan's FSA issued another warning. Singapore's Monetary Authority placed Binance on its investor alert list.

### 3. Zhao's Dilemma and the Crossroads

The summer of 2021. Late one night, Zhao sat alone in his hotel room in Singapore.

"What should I do?" he asked himself. "Keep fighting, or concede?"

In the eyes of an idealist, he should stand his ground. In the eyes of a pragmatist, he had to bend.

"I cannot sacrifice the interests of our users for the sake of ideals alone," Zhao wrote in his journal. "Binance does not belong to me alone. It belongs to the millions of users who have placed their trust in us."

He made his decision: "We will transform — from a startup that prioritized speed and growth, into an institution that prioritizes compliance and security."

### 4. The Pain of Compliance Transformation

2021 to 2022, and Binance began its painful pivot to compliance.

First, the KYC process was strengthened. Every user was required to complete rigorous identity verification. Next came the cleanup of high-risk markets — Binance shuttered certain services in China, Turkey, Iran, and elsewhere.

"I received death threats," Zhao later admitted. "Some said I had betrayed the spirit of cryptocurrency. But my job is to protect the interests of the many."

Then came the hiring of compliance executives. Zhao recruited a slate of senior leaders from traditional finance and regulatory agencies.

The cost of transformation was staggering. Binance's annual compliance expenditure surged from tens of millions to hundreds of millions of dollars.

"This was the hardest period," Zhao recalled. "I watched our market share shrink. I watched competitors overtake us. But it was the right road."

### 5. The Winter of 2022

2022, and the cryptocurrency market entered a "crypto winter."

The collapse of Terra/Luna triggered a cascade of failures. Then came the fall of FTX.

In November 2022, Sam Bankman-Fried's cryptocurrency empire crumbled.

"This is one of the darkest moments in cryptocurrency history," Zhao wrote on Twitter.

FTX's collapse brought fresh challenges for Binance — a crisis of confidence first, then a run-risk. Binance endured a massive wave of withdrawals, with billions of dollars flowing out in the days following FTX's collapse.

"This was a stress test for us," Zhao said.

Binance passed. Every withdrawal request was honored.

"We had sufficient reserves," Zhao said. "We have never put our users' assets at risk through reckless investments."

## Part Four: The Turning Point — Reborn from the Ashes, a New King Ascends

### 1. America's Iron Fist

2023, and the regulatory tempest reached its crescendo.

In March, the U.S. Commodity Futures Trading Commission (CFTC) filed a lawsuit against Binance and Zhao. In June, the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) joined the fray, charging Binance and Zhao with securities law violations.

"This is the most severe legal challenge we have ever faced," Zhao said at an internal meeting.

He assembled one of the nation's top legal teams. Legal fees quickly climbed into the tens of millions of dollars.

### 2. The Settlement and the Exit

November 2023, and Binance reached a settlement agreement with the U.S. Department of Justice.

- Binance would pay $4.3 billion in penalties — the largest fine in cryptocurrency history.
- Zhao would resign as CEO and pay $50 million in personal fines.
- Zhao would be barred from participating in Binance's management for three years.

"This is a painful but necessary decision," Zhao wrote in his resignation letter. "I made mistakes, and I must take responsibility for them."

At his final all-hands meeting as CEO, Zhao delivered a farewell address:

"Six years ago, we started in this very conference room. Today, we have hundreds of millions of users and thousands of employees. But more importantly, we have proven something — that cryptocurrency can serve the masses, that decentralized finance is possible."

"I am about to leave, but I am not sad. Binance no longer belongs to me alone. It belongs to each and every one of you. Creating value for users — that is the only thing that matters."

### 3. The Richard Teng Era

Zhao's successor, Richard Teng, was a seasoned veteran from the world of traditional finance.

Upon taking the helm, Teng enacted a wave of reforms: strengthening the global compliance team, improving transparency, and diversifying the business.

"We want to become a true fintech company," Teng said, "not merely an exchange."

Under Teng's leadership, Binance grew more resilient. Market share declined somewhat, but profitability remained strong.

### 4. Zhao's Redemption

April 2024, and Zhao walked into a federal courtroom in Seattle.

The judge ultimately sentenced him to four months in prison.

"Given the defendant's acceptance of responsibility, given Binance's enormous fine, and given the defendant's contributions to this industry, I find four months to be an appropriate sentence," the judge declared.

While incarcerated, Zhao read extensively about blockchain and artificial intelligence, planning the projects he would pursue upon release.

"Prison is a wonderful place for reflection," he wrote. "I finally had time to think about some fundamental questions."

His conclusion: education.

September 2024, and Zhao was released from prison. He did not return to Binance. Instead, he founded a new project — Giggle Academy. A free online education platform leveraging AI and blockchain technology to deliver educational resources to children in impoverished regions around the world.

"I am already financially free," he said at the project's launch event. "I no longer need to work for money. Now, I will work for meaning."

### 5. The Rise of New Forces

While Binance weathered its storms, profound changes were reshaping the cryptocurrency world.

First, there was the rise of Solana. This high-performance public chain staged a powerful comeback in 2023–2024, becoming the second-largest public chain after Ethereum.

Then came the fusion of AI and cryptocurrency. Projects like Fetch.ai and SingularityNET sought to use blockchain to solve core problems in the AI field.

Then came the tokenization of real-world assets (RWA). Real estate, equities, bonds, and other traditional financial assets began migrating onto the blockchain.

"This industry never stops innovating," Zhao said in an interview after his release. "Even in the worst of times, developers keep building."

## Part Five: Reflections — The Coming of Age of the Crypto World

### 1. From Savagery to Civilization

2024, and the cryptocurrency industry underwent a transformation from wild growth to measured maturity.

Binance's story epitomized this shift. Under Zhao's leadership, Binance had embodied the wildest, most aggressive edge of the crypto industry. Under Teng's stewardship, Binance began learning to coexist with the traditional world.

"This is not betrayal — it is evolution," Zhao said. "When an industry grows up, it must learn to take responsibility."

### 2. Balancing Regulation and Innovation

The central question facing the crypto world is how to balance regulation with innovation.

"We need to find a third path," Zhao said. "A regulatory framework that protects consumers while still encouraging innovation."

His proposed solution was "tiered risk-based regulation." For straightforward trading and holding activities, regulation should be light. For complex financial derivatives, regulation should be stringent.

### 3. The True Value of Technology

"Not speculation, not gambling," Zhao said. "But financial freedom, technology empowerment, and the connection of the global economy."

In developing nations, cryptocurrency carries even greater weight. For residents of Argentina and Turkey, it is a harbor of refuge against runaway inflation. For migrant workers in Africa and Southeast Asia, it is a low-cost channel for remitting money home.

"That is what we were after from the very beginning," Zhao said.

### 4. Looking Ahead

2024, and Bitcoin ETFs were approved in the United States — marking the formal convergence of traditional finance and cryptocurrency.

"This is not an ending, but a beginning," Zhao said. "Over the next decade, we will see far greater integration. The boundary between traditional finance and decentralized finance will grow increasingly blurred."

## Epilogue: The Winds Never Cease, the World Endures

Late autumn 2024, Dubai.

Changpeng Zhao stood on the balcony of the Burj Al Arab, gazing out over the azure waters of the Persian Gulf.

His Giggle Academy had been running for a year, serving over a million students.

His phone buzzed. A message from Richard Teng: Binance had just celebrated its seventh anniversary, with its user base surpassing 200 million.

Zhao smiled as he sent his congratulations. He was no longer CEO — but he remained one of Binance's largest shareholders.

"The storms of the crypto world will never cease," he murmured into the night sky. "Old heroes will fall, new heroes will rise. But the dream — of freedom, of innovation, of connecting the world — that dream will endure forever."

Outside the window, the wind stirred. The winds may shift, the storms may rage — but the world goes on.
