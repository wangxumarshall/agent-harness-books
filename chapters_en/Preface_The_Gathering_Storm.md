## Prologue: The Rainy Night Lehman Brothers Fell

September 15, 2008, Monday. Hong Kong.

This day was destined to be etched into history. Lehman Brothers, the fourth-largest investment bank in America with 158 years of history, officially filed for bankruptcy protection. This was the largest bankruptcy in American history, and the climax of the global financial crisis of 2008.

On the other side of the globe, in Hong Kong, an old man sat alone in the study of his Mid-Levels mansion, gazing through the floor-to-ceiling windows at the gloom hovering over Victoria Harbour.

His name was Zhou Shiming, seventy-eight years old, a "veteran of the financial world" revered by all in the industry.

Zhou Shiming's life was a living chronicle of finance. Born into a banking family on the Shanghai Bund, he moved to Hong Kong with his family as a boy, studied economics at Cambridge University in his youth, then pursued financial engineering at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology. His career spanned more than half a century — from traditional banking, through the collapse of the Bretton Woods system, oil crises, Black Monday, the Asian financial crisis, the dot-com bubble… He had witnessed countless cycles of boom and bust, survived countless heart-pounding trading moments.

But tonight, even for an old man like him who had weathered countless storms, an indescribable emotion stirred within — shock, worry, excitement, and anticipation all tangled together. Like an old captain on a sea before a storm, catching an unfamiliar scent in the air.

Outside the window, autumn rain fell softly. The lights of Victoria Harbour blurred in the rain curtain, and the towering skyscrapers — the Bank of China Tower, the HSBC Headquarters, the Cheung Kong Center — stood silent in the darkness, like solemn giants overlooking this city that never slept.

But Zhou Shiming knew: those steel titans, symbols of financial hegemony, were trembling at this very moment.

On the wall of his study hung a massive flat-screen television, broadcasting Bloomberg's real-time news. The screen showed New York's Times Square; outside Lehman Brothers' headquarters, employees walked out through revolving doors carrying cardboard boxes, their faces filled with bewilderment and despair. The camera captured a middle-aged trader, choking back tears before the lens: "Thirty years. I gave thirty years to this company, and now there's nothing."

Zhou Shiming lifted the whisky from the side table and took a small sip. The amber liquid slid down his throat, bringing a warm burn.

"So this is the wheel of history turning," he murmured to himself, his voice low and hoarse. "The Great Depression of 1929, Black Monday in 1987, the Asian Storm of 1997, the dot-com bubble of 2000… Every time, people said 'this time is different,' but every time, the ending was the same. Greed, fear, revelry, collapse. Human nature never changes."

He rose, walked to the window, hands clasped behind his back, staring out at the rainy night.

The study was adorned with traces of history. On the walls hung several black-and-white photographs documenting pivotal moments of his life: a group photo with his mentor at Cambridge, a memento from his first year working on Wall Street, commemorative photographs from Hong Kong's return financial negotiations… Most prominently displayed was a photo of him with an elderly man with white hair, captioned: "Amsterdam, 1995, with David Chaum."

That was David Chaum, pioneer of digital currency, inventor of eCash, an idealist who challenged the financial system with mathematics and cryptography.

Zhou Shiming's gaze lingered on that photograph for a moment, his eyes flickering with a complex expression.

"Chaum, do you see this?" he said softly, as if speaking to the person in the photo. "The day you prophesied has finally arrived. The currency revolution you dreamed of may be about to begin."

He turned, walked to his desk, and opened his computer. On the screen was a cryptography mailing list page — metzdowd.com, one of the most prestigious discussion groups in the cryptographic community, bringing together the world's top cryptographers, computer scientists, and cryptography enthusiasts.

Zhou Shiming browsed this list every day, even though he had been retired for years, even though most of the mathematical discussions were beyond his comprehension. But he maintained this habit, because he knew that within this seemingly dry academic community, forces capable of changing the world might be gestating.

He scrolled through, browsing recent discussion topics. Most posts were technical details — new cryptographic algorithms, protocol vulnerability discussions, academic paper reviews… But suddenly, one title caught his eye:

**"Bitcoin P2P e-cash paper"**

The poster had used a Japanized name: Satoshi Nakamoto.

Zhou Shiming's heart quickened.

He clicked to open the email. Nine pages of a PDF document appeared on screen. The title: *Bitcoin: A Peer-to-Peer Electronic Cash System*.

"Bitcoin… a peer-to-peer electronic cash system…" Zhou Shiming read the title aloud softly, a gleam flickering in his eyes.

He began reading the paper, word by word, like an archaeologist deciphering ancient inscriptions. As he read deeper, his hands began to tremble slightly.

"Distributed timestamp server… proof of work… longest-chain rule… solution to the double-spend problem…"

Each technical term was like a stone dropped into the still lake of his heart, sending ripples spreading outward.

"This is the answer," he murmured, his voice trembling with excitement. "This is the answer Chaum dreamed of, the answer that Wei Dai, Nick Szabo, and Hal Finney had been searching for. Decentralized, no banks, no government — only mathematics and cryptography. Satoshi Nakamoto did it. He really did it."

He released the mouse and leaned back in his chair, falling into a long silence.

Outside, the sound of rain seemed to grow clearer; each drop against the glass felt like it was knocking on his heart.

He thought of years past, of heated discussions on the cryptography mailing list, of DigiCash's glory and downfall, of countless nights burning for ideals.

"Digital currency is like Pandora's box," he said softly. "Chaum tried to open it, but he was too early — the world wasn't ready. Now, perhaps the time has come. But once this box is opened, it can never be closed again. What lies inside may be hope, or it may be disaster."

Zhou Shiming stood up and walked to the window once more.

The rain had eased, but the sky remained overcast. On the distant sea, several cargo ships drifted slowly past, their lights flickering in the darkness like signals from the lost.

"A new era is about to begin," he said, his voice low but firm. "I don't know if it's good or bad, but I know this: it will be an unprecedented storm. The crypto world — this jianghu that is about to be born — will change everything."

He turned and looked at the computer on his desk, still displaying the whitepaper on the screen.

"Perhaps I should record all of this," he thought. "From the perspective of an old veteran, telling the story of this world — its birth, growth, madness, and maturation. This is not a simple story about technology; it is a story about human nature — about greed and ideals, betrayal and loyalty, the struggle for power and the pursuit of freedom."

Zhou Shiming walked back to his desk and opened a new document. His hands hovered above the keyboard.

"Where should I begin?" he asked himself.

"Let's begin with the dawn of cryptography," he decided. "Let's begin with that night in 1983 when David Chaum wrote the first line of code for eCash. That is the source of all this, the true starting point of crypto history."

He began typing, line after line, building a bridge to the past with words.

Outside, the autumn rain continued. But in the old man's heart, an even greater storm was brewing.

## Part One: The Long Night Before the Birth of the Crypto World

### I. The Nature and Predicament of Money

To understand the story of the crypto world, we must first return to a fundamental question: What is money?

This question seems simple, yet it has puzzled humanity for thousands of years. From shells to gold and silver, from paper money to electronic bookkeeping, the forms of money have constantly evolved, but their core functions have remained unchanged: a unit of account, a medium of exchange, a store of value.

Zhou Shiming often used a simple metaphor to explain the nature of money.

"Imagine a primitive tribe," he said. "There are hunters, farmers, and craftsmen. The hunter catches a rabbit and wants to trade it for the farmer's wheat, but the farmer doesn't want a rabbit — he wants the craftsman's clay pot. What do you do then? Without a commonly accepted intermediary, the trade cannot be completed. That intermediary is money."

But the modern monetary system is far more complex than this simple metaphor suggests.

In modern society, money is essentially a form of credit — you believe that the paper or that number represents real value, that others will accept it as a means of payment, that the government will maintain its purchasing power. This trust is the cornerstone of the entire financial system.

"But the problem is," Zhou Shiming pointed out, "that this trust is fragile. Governments can overissue currency, banks can abuse their power, and financial institutions can manipulate markets. The 2008 financial crisis was, at its core, a crisis of trust. People discovered that the institutions they trusted, the 'too big to fail' banks, were full of greed and fraud."

Lehman Brothers' bankruptcy was merely the tip of the iceberg. In that crisis, global stock markets lost over $30 trillion, hundreds of millions lost their jobs, and tens of millions lost their homes. While government rescue measures stabilized the financial system, they exposed an unsettling truth: the traditional financial system is built on inequality.

"When banks get into trouble, governments bail them out with taxpayers' money," Zhou Shiming said. "But when ordinary people fall into trouble, who bails them out? This inequality is systemic and deeply entrenched."

It was against this backdrop that the concept of digital currency began to attract increasing attention.

### II. The Awakening of Cryptography

The dream of digital currency can be traced back to the awakening of cryptography.

In 1976, Whitfield Diffie and Martin Hellman published the historic paper "New Directions in Cryptography," introducing the concept of public-key cryptography. Before this, cryptography was primarily the domain of the military, inaccessible to ordinary people who had no access to truly secure encryption technology.

"Public-key cryptography is a double-edged sword," Zhou Shiming explained. "It can be used to protect privacy, but also to create new trust mechanisms. In traditional cryptography, two communicating parties must first exchange keys through some secure method — it's a chicken-and-egg problem. Public-key cryptography solved this — you can publish your public key, and anyone can use it to send you encrypted messages, but only you, with your private key, can decrypt them."

This technology laid the technical foundation for digital currency. If people could communicate securely using public and private keys, why couldn't they use them to securely transfer value?

In 1983, David Chaum proposed the concept of eCash, the first demonstration of how cryptography could create anonymous digital currency. Although eCash ultimately failed, it proved such a possibility existed.

"Chaum was a true idealist," Zhou Shiming recalled. "He believed technology could protect privacy, that mathematics could defend freedom. In that era, such idealism was rare and precious."

Over the following two decades and more, generation after generation of cryptographers, programmers, and idealists continued to explore. Wei Dai proposed the concept of b-money, Nick Szabo designed Bit Gold, Hal Finney implemented the Reusable Proof of Work (RPOW) system… Each attempt moved one step forward, each failure accumulated valuable experience.

"These people were all pioneers," Zhou Shiming said. "They walked in darkness, unable to see the endpoint, uncertain whether their efforts would yield results. But they pressed on, because they believed that even if they themselves couldn't see the light, their work would illuminate the path for future generations."

### III. The Rise of the Internet and the Budding of the Digital Economy

In the 1990s, the internet evolved from an academic network to commercial applications, fundamentally transforming the way human society operated.

Email replaced letters, webpages replaced newspapers, and e-commerce began to sprout. But behind all this prosperity, one problem remained unsolved: how to make secure, private, and instant payments in the digital world?

Credit cards were the first solution, but they were riddled with problems. First, credit card payments relied on intermediaries like banks, adding transaction costs and time. Second, credit card payments were completely transparent — every transaction was recorded and tracked, leaving users' privacy unprotected. Third, credit card systems were vulnerable, susceptible to fraud and hacker attacks.

"The internet was designed to be open and decentralized," Zhou Shiming pointed out. "Anyone can create a website, anyone can publish information. But the payment system is centralized, closed, controlled by a few banks and credit card companies. This mismatch is obvious."

Online payment platforms like PayPal partially solved this problem, but they remained centralized, still dependent on the traditional banking system, and still faced regulatory and security risks.

"People needed a currency that truly belonged to the internet," Zhou Shiming said. "A currency as private as cash, as instant as email, as decentralized as the internet itself. That was the dream of digital currency."

But this dream remained unrealized until the appearance of Satoshi Nakamoto.

### IV. 2008: The Perfect Storm

2008 was a special year.

The global economy fell into crisis, and public trust in the traditional financial system hit rock bottom. At the same time, the internet had matured, cryptographic technology had become sufficiently powerful, and the open-source software culture had taken root. All the conditions were in place, waiting only for that crucial breakthrough.

"History is often rewritten at moments like these," Zhou Shiming said. "When old systems fail, when new technologies mature, when social needs become urgent, change happens. 2008 was one such moment."

September 15: Lehman Brothers went bankrupt. That same month, Satoshi Nakamoto began writing the Bitcoin whitepaper. October 31: the whitepaper was officially published. January 3, 2009: the first block of Bitcoin was mined, officially inaugurating the era of digital currency.

"This was no coincidence," Zhou Shiming said. "Satoshi Nakamoto chose this moment to publish Bitcoin with deep intent. In the Genesis Block, he embedded a message: 'The Times 03/Jan/2009 Chancellor on brink of second bailout for banks.' This was an indictment of the traditional financial system and a declaration of the new era."

The birth of Bitcoin was not merely a technological innovation; it was an ideological manifesto. It proclaimed a possibility: that currency could exist beyond government and bank control, that value could be transferred without trusted intermediaries, and that the financial system could become more open, transparent, and fair.

"Of course, this is only the ideal," Zhou Shiming added. "Reality is always far more complicated than ideals. But what matters is that the ideal was raised, and someone began to put it into practice. This would change everything."

## Part Two: The Crypto World — A Jianghu About to Be Born

### I. Defining the Jianghu

In Zhou Shiming's eyes, the crypto world is not merely a technical community or an investment market — it is a *jianghu*.

"What is the jianghu?" he often asked. "The jianghu is not a geographical concept, but a form of social existence. It is a world outside the mainstream, a land where rules are unwritten, a wilderness where opportunity and danger coexist. In ancient times, the jianghu had its school rivalries, martial arts grandmasters, reclusive masters, and also charlatans. Some fought for ideals, some went mad for wealth, some rose to glory, some fell into ruin."

The crypto world is precisely such a jianghu.

It is a decentralized network with no clear boundaries, no unified leadership, no fixed rules. Anyone can join, anyone can leave. There is no identity verification, no background checks — only public keys and private keys, only code and consensus.

"In this jianghu, a sixteen-year-old high school student may understand digital currency better than a veteran Wall Street trader," Zhou Shiming said. "An anonymous programmer may have more influence than a Fortune 500 CEO. This is a world where technical prowess determines status, where code determines rules."

But this jianghu is also dangerous.

The absence of regulation means the absence of protection. Scammers can abscond with funds easily, hackers can steal massive assets, and project teams can fabricate lies to deceive investors. Price volatility is extreme; overnight fortune and financial ruin are separated by only a thin line.

"The crypto world is like a magnifying glass," Zhou Shiming said. "It amplifies both the best and worst of human nature. Here, you can see the purest idealism, and also the most naked greed. You can see the most sincere community spirit, and also the most despicable betrayal."

### II. The Essence of Turbulent Winds and Clouds

The phrase "turbulent winds and gathering clouds" (fengyun) is Zhou Shiming's most apt description of the crypto world.

The wind rises from beneath the duckweed; the clouds gather at the edge of the horizon. No one can predict what will happen in the next second, no one can know where the next legend or tragedy will unfold.

"Traditional financial markets are like the ocean," Zhou Shiming said. "Although there are storms, there are certain patterns to follow. You can check weather forecasts, observe ocean currents, predict seasonal changes. But the crypto world is like clouds and wind in the sky — ever-changing and unpredictable."

This "turbulent" quality stems from several factors:

First, the rapid evolution of technology. Just a few years after Bitcoin was born, innovations like Ethereum, smart contracts, Decentralized Finance (DeFi), and Non-Fungible Tokens (NFTs) emerged. Every new technology could reshape the entire landscape; every new protocol could overturn the old order.

Second, globalization and round-the-clock trading. The crypto world never sleeps. When Asian markets close, European markets are just opening; when European markets rest, American markets are buzzing with activity. News and sentiment spread globally in an instant, and price movements never cease.

Third, regulatory uncertainty. Governments worldwide hold varying attitudes toward digital currency — some embrace it, some ban it, some wait and see. Policy changes can trigger violent market volatility in an instant.

Fourth, the amplification of human nature. In this unregulated jianghu without barriers, greed and fear are infinitely amplified. Fear Of Missing Out (FOMO) and Fear, Uncertainty, and Doubt (FUD) drive market sentiment, creating泡沫 after泡沫 and崩溃 after崩溃.

"This is the charm of the crypto world, and also its danger," Zhou Shiming said. "It makes you feel as though you are living on the crest of history's wave, where every decision could change your destiny. But this also means enormous risk, because no one can truly control the winds and clouds, no one can truly predict the future."

### III. The Metaphor of Pandora's Box

Zhou Shiming often used "Pandora's box" to describe digital currency.

In Greek mythology, Pandora was the first woman, given a box (actually a jar) and told not to open it. But curiosity drove her to disobey, and from the box flew all evils — disease, suffering, greed, envy — leaving only hope at the bottom.

"Digital currency is such a box," Zhou Shiming said. "It contains the power to change the world, but also the potential for destruction. It can be used to liberate humanity, giving people true financial freedom; but it can also be used to evade regulation, launder money, evade taxes, and fund crime. It is a tool of freedom, and also a catalyst for chaos."

This duality is an essential characteristic of digital currency.

On one hand, it provides opportunities for those excluded from the traditional financial system. Billions of people globally lack bank accounts and cannot access basic financial services. Digital currency can allow them to participate in the global economy through their mobile phones, gaining access to savings, lending, and payment便利.

On the other hand, it does provide new tools for criminals. Dark web drug trades, ransomware black markets, money laundering and tax evasion… These dark sides are real and cannot be ignored.

"Technology is neutral," Zhou Shiming said. "Just as fire can warm us or set fires, a knife can cook or harm. The key lies in how it is used. We cannot reject technology itself simply because it is misused, but neither can we ignore the risks it brings."

He believed the real challenge was finding a balance — enjoying the freedom and convenience digital currency brings while controlling the risks and harms it generates. This is a long-term process requiring joint efforts in technology, law, education, and culture.

"Pandora's box has been opened," he said. "We can no longer close it. What we can do is learn to coexist with the forces within the box, and use hope to combat the evils that have escaped."

## Part Three: The Structure and Story of This Book

### I. The Thread of History

This book will trace the history of the crypto world from its birth to maturity along the axis of time.

We will begin with the dawn of cryptography, revisiting the dreams and shattering of David Chaum and eCash. That was the first wave of digital currency; though it ultimately failed, it sowed the seeds and left a valuable intellectual legacy.

Then we will enter the Bitcoin era. Satoshi Nakamoto's whitepaper, the fervor of the first miners, the rise of early exchanges, and that legendary pizza transaction — ten thousand bitcoins for two pizzas. This was the Genesis age of the crypto world, the era of purest idealism.

Next, we will witness the rise of mining. From CPUs to GPUs, from FPGAs to ASICs, how did mining evolve from a geek's hobby into a multi-billion-dollar industry? We will tell the legendary story of Friedcat, his rise, mysterious disappearance, and the eternal riddle he left behind for the crypto world.

The story of the dark web cannot be avoided either. The rise and fall of Silk Road, Ross Ulbricht's ideals and crimes, and how Bitcoin first intersected with the dark side of mainstream society. This is a complex story about freedom and boundaries, innovation and crime.

The birth of Ethereum was another turning point. Vitalik Buterin's visionary concept, the smart contract revolution, and the madness and泡沫 of the Initial Coin Offering (ICO) frenzy. This was the crypto world's transition from a single currency to a diverse ecosystem.

We will also tell the story of the exchange wars. The collapse of Mt.Gox, the rise of new-generation exchanges like Binance and Coinbase, and the paradox of building trust in a jianghu plagued by crises of trust.

DeFi Summer, the NFT craze, institutional investors entering the market, national embrace and suppression… Every chapter is a magnificent span of history, every story worthy of remembrance.

### II. A Gallery of Characters

The history of the crypto world was written by people.

In this book, you will encounter a cast of diverse characters:

**The Idealists**: People like David Chaum, Satoshi Nakamoto, and Vitalik Buterin, who fought not for wealth but for the ideal of changing the world. They used code and mathematics to build new orders, using technological innovation to challenge established power structures.

**The Entrepreneurs**: People like CZ (Zhao Changpeng), Brian Armstrong, and Wu Jihan, who saw opportunity in chaos and built empires amid uncertainty. They are the warlords of this jianghu, the definers of rules.

**The Speculators**: From early miners to later hedge funds, from retail investors to institutions, attracted by tales of fortune, chasing profits in price swings. They are participants in the market, and also the creators of泡沫 and the bearers of their bursts.

**The Scoundrels and Criminals**: Unfortunately, the crypto world also attracted numerous wrongdoers. From Bitconnect's Ponzi scheme to various rug-pull scams, from dark web transactions to money laundering networks, these people exploited the uncertainties of new technology to prey on others.

**The Regulators and Politicians**: From early hostility to later ambiguity, from China's comprehensive ban to El Salvador's legal tender adoption, how have governments and regulatory bodies responded to this phenomenon that challenges traditional authority?

**The Ordinary Users**: Last but not least, the ordinary people who came to the crypto world seeking opportunity, learning knowledge, and participating in communities. They are the foundation of this ecosystem, the true protagonists of the story.

"Everyone who enters the crypto world has their own story," Zhou Shiming said. "Some came for wealth, some for freedom, some for technology, some merely out of curiosity. But regardless, once they enter this jianghu, they are swept into the storm and become part of history."

### III. The Lessons of Turbulent Winds

Through these stories, we hope to answer some deeper questions:

**Technology and Society**: How does a revolutionary technology change society? And how does society shape technological development? Digital currency is not merely a technical issue; it touches on economics, politics, sociology, and ethics.

**Trust and Decentralization**: In a world where individuals act out of self-interest, how do we build systems that require no trust? Is decentralization truly the solution? Or have we merely replaced one power structure with another?

**Freedom and Responsibility**: Are privacy and anonymity fundamental rights, or covers for crime? How do we protect individual freedom while preventing the misuse of technology?

**Wealth and Value**: When Bitcoin's price rises from a few cents to tens of thousands of dollars, when NFTs sell for millions, does this represent real value, or pure speculation? What is the nature of value?

**Innovation and Tradition**: The crypto world has challenged the foundations of the traditional financial system — but is this progress or chaos? The old order has its flaws, but is the new order necessarily better?

"I don't claim to have answers to all these questions," Zhou Shiming said. "In fact, I think many questions don't have simple answers. But by telling these stories, by understanding these figures and this history, we can at least better understand the complexity of the issues and form more informed judgments."

## Part Four: Writing Style and Perspective

### I. The Genre of Yan Yi (Historical Fiction)

This book narrates the history of the crypto world in the style of *yan yi*.

*Yan yi* is a genre of classical Chinese literature, the most famous example being *Romance of the Three Kingdoms* (*Sanguo Yanyi*). It is based on historical facts but incorporates extensive literary embellishment — reconstructed dialogues, scene descriptions, character psychology, and dramatic plot arrangements.

Why choose this genre?

"First of all, the history of the crypto world itself reads like a yan yi novel," Zhou Shiming explained. "Full of legendary color, dramatic twists, heroes and villains, rises and falls. To narrate it in plain, straightforward fashion would actually rob it of its charm."

"Second, many details can no longer be verified. Who is Satoshi Nakamoto? Where did Friedcat go? These riddles may never have answers. The yan yi form gives us the freedom to plausibly imagine those unknown details, to construct a coherent narrative."

"Most importantly, I want this book to be readable and engaging. The story of the crypto world is too magnificent to deserve telling in any other way."

But this book is not a novel. All major events are based on real history; all main characters are real people (except for Zhou Shiming himself as narrator, and some composite characters). Technical details have been carefully verified; timelines are essentially accurate.

"You can think of it as a 'dramatized recreation of real events,'" Zhou Shiming said. "Like *The Social Network* to Facebook's history, or *The Big Short* to the financial crisis. It captures the spirit of the story, even if some details are fictional."

### II. The Omniscient Perspective and Zhou Shiming's Role

This book employs a third-person omniscient perspective, but with a special narrative device: Zhou Shiming as an "old veteran" offering observations and commentary.

Zhou Shiming is a fictional character, but his perspective is authentic. He represents those early adopters who witnessed the entire development of the crypto world, the cypherpunks who lurked in cryptography communities for years, and the investors who straddled both traditional finance and the emerging crypto world.

Through his eyes, we can see connections between events, understand the脉络 of technological development, and gain insight into the complexity of human nature.

"I am an observer, and also a participant," Zhou Shiming said in his narration. "I witnessed it all happen, sometimes joining in, more often merely watching from the sidelines. This gives me both an insider's insight and an outsider's objectivity."

His commentary runs throughout the book, sometimes explaining technical concepts, sometimes providing historical context, sometimes sharing personal reflections and warnings.

"I hope readers can, through me, better understand the significance of this story," he said. "It's not merely a story about technology and money — it's a story about human nature."

### III. Advice for the Reader

Before beginning this journey, Zhou Shiming has some advice for the reader:

**Keep an Open Mind**: The crypto world is a topic full of controversy. Some see it as salvation; others see it as a scam. As you read this book, please set aside your preconceptions for the moment, and try to understand the reasonableness of differing viewpoints.

**Distinguish Facts from Opinions**: The book contains both accounts of historical facts and analyses and judgments of events. Please learn to distinguish between the two and form your own judgments.

**Think Critically**: Do not blindly believe the viewpoints of any character in this book (including the narrator, Zhou Shiming). Questioning everything is at the core of the crypto spirit, and should also be your attitude when reading this book.

**Enjoy the Story**: Most importantly, enjoy this journey. The history of the crypto world is a magnificent epic — with tragedy, comedy, suspense, and surprise. Relax, and enjoy the story.

## Epilogue: The Curtain Rises, the Storm Awaits

Now, let us return to that rainy night in Hong Kong.

The old man Zhou Shiming was still typing in his study, fingers dancing across the keyboard, line after line of text appearing on the screen.

Outside the window, the rain had stopped. The first light of dawn pierced through the clouds, illuminating Victoria Harbour. The sea sparkled, and the silhouettes of skyscrapers grew clear in the morning light.

The old man stopped typing, stood up, walked to the window, and took a deep breath of the fresh morning air.

"A new day has begun," he said. "A new era has begun too."

He turned and looked at the words he had just written on the computer screen.

"I don't know who will read this book," he murmured. "Perhaps a young person who has just entered the crypto world, drawn by tales of instant wealth. Perhaps a curious onlooker who wants to understand this strange new world. Perhaps a veteran looking back on this crazy journey."

"Regardless of who you are, I want to say to you: Welcome to the crypto world. Welcome to this jianghu filled with opportunity and traps."

"Please remember, this is not merely a story about technology and money. This is a story about human nature — about greed and ideals, betrayal and loyalty, the struggle for power and the pursuit of freedom."

"The crypto world is like an ancient jianghu — with school rivalries, martial arts grandmasters, reclusive masters, and also charlatans. Some fight for ideals, some go mad for wealth, some rise to glory in this jianghu, and some fall into ruin here."

"Who will have the last laugh in these turbulent winds? Only time will tell."

"But whatever happens, this is a story worth telling. This is a story of our era."

The old man sat back down and clicked the "Save" button.

"Very well," he said. "Let us begin. From the dawn of cryptography. From that night when David Chaum began. That is the source of all this — the true starting point of crypto history."

He gazed out the window. The morning sun had fully risen, golden light spilling across the entire city.

"The jianghu stretches far, and the winds and clouds never rest. To be continued."
