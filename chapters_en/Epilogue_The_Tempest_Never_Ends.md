## Prologue: Whispers in the Twilight

Hong Kong, late autumn 2024.

The sunset over Victoria Harbour sinks into the sea, dyeing the horizon blood-red. Sea wind howls through the skyscrapers. In an old mansion on The Peak, Zhou Shiming — past ninety now — sits in a rattan chair, his gaze passing through the sea of clouds to the horizon.

On the antique laptop on the tea table, the Bitcoin market price glows on the screen — sixty-eight thousand US dollars. The number flickers, its green light in the twilight.

"It's back up again," the old man murmurs to himself. "How many burials, how many resurrections. This must be its fate."

Outside the window, a withered leaf is caught by the wind, spiraling through the air before drifting slowly down. The old man watches the leaf, his gaze growing distant.

"Chaum, do you see this?" he whispers to the void. "The digital currency you dreamed of has grown into a towering tree. Its branches have broken in countless storms, but its roots go deeper and deeper."

Only the old man's voice echoes in the room. In this moment, those departed souls seem to gather all around — David Chaum, Hal Finney, the mysterious Satoshi Nakamoto, and the heroes and villains who rose and fell in the crypto storms.

"Sixteen years now," the old man sighs. "Counting from that autumn of 2008. When Satoshi published the whitepaper, who could have imagined this day would come?"

He stands and looks down at this city where he has spent nearly a lifetime. Hong Kong's nightscape is unfolding, ten thousand lights like stars scattered across the earth.

"What a grand drama," he says softly. "What a fine performance indeed."

The old man takes a thick notebook from the bookshelf — his private diary, recording the crypto world's winds and clouds through the years. He flips open to the first page, where his sturdy handwriting reads:

"To future readers: The crypto world is like a martial arts realm, where heroes rise in great numbers, yet tragedy follows close behind. May those who come after use this book as a mirror, finding balance between greed and ideals."

"It's time to write the ending," he tells himself. "To draw a final curtain on this grand drama — or rather, a new beginning."

He returns to his desk, opens the document, his fingers hovering over the keyboard. Outside the window, the wind rises, dark clouds gathering from the horizon, blocking the last rays of sunset.

"The tempest never ends," he whispers. "The realm endures forever."

## Chapter One: Pioneers Past

### I. The Vigil Keepers of the Cryptography Temple

Zhou Shiming's gaze falls upon an old photograph on the wall. It was taken in the 1990s, showing a dozen young people standing before a massive mainframe, their faces alight with idealistic radiance. At the center stands David Chaum — the blonde, blue-eyed founding father of the digital currency dream.

"Chaum, how are you?" the old man asks the photograph softly. "In that world without pain, are you still chasing dreams?"

David Chaum passed away in 2023, at sixty-eight. His death drew brief mourning in the cryptography community, but it was soon buried beneath the crypto world's new hotspots — another Meme coin surging, another exchange hacked, another country's regulatory policy shifting. The torrent of news flows ever onward, and the departed can only be interred in the depths of memory.

But Zhou Shiming remembers. He remembers the young people burning the midnight oil in Amsterdam's underground laboratory, the idealism ablaze in their eyes, the excitement in Chaum's voice when he first described the eCash concept.

"We will create a fully anonymous digital currency," Chaum said then, his voice trembling with emotion. "Governments cannot track it, banks cannot control it, everyone can freely use their own money — just like cash."

What a beautiful dream that was. In that dream, technology would become a tool for human freedom, cryptography would shatter the chains of finance, ordinary people would possess the same financial liberty as the powerful.

But between dreams and reality, there always lies an unbridgeable chasm.

Chaum's DigiCash ultimately failed. Resistance from banking giants, technological limitations, market indifference — these factors together buried the first digital currency dream. Chaum left America in defeat, returning to the Netherlands to live a semi-reclusive life.

"I came too early," Chaum said in a rare later interview. "The world of the 1990s wasn't ready for digital currency. The internet wasn't widespread enough, cryptography wasn't mature enough, people's minds weren't open enough."

But the seeds he planted quietly took root in the darkness.

Wei Dai — the mysterious B-money proposer — articulated the concept of decentralized ledger in 1998. His true identity remains a mystery to this day. Regardless, his ideas influenced Bitcoin's later design.

Nick Szabo — the Bit Gold proposer — described in 2005 a digital currency system based on Proof of Work. His design was remarkably close to Bitcoin, lacking only one final crucial piece: a solution to the double-spend problem.

Hal Finney — the true cryptographic hero — created the Reusable Proofs of Work (RPOW) system in 2004, demonstrating the feasibility of a proof-of-work currency. He later became one of Bitcoin's earliest participants, completing the first Bitcoin transaction with Satoshi Nakamoto in 2009.

"When I ran the Bitcoin software, it worked like magic," Finney later recalled. "I knew this was a historic moment."

Finney passed away in 2014 from Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis (ALS). In his final moments, he donated his Bitcoin holdings to the ALS Research Fund. Even in the face of death, he remained true to his idealistic nature.

"These pioneers were the vigil keepers of the cryptography temple," Zhou Shiming writes in his notebook. "Through the long night, they guarded the flickering flame. They did not see the dawn, but without their vigil, dawn would never have come."

The old man's eyes grow moist. He thinks of those youthful nights, the fierce debates on the cryptography mailing list, the dreams of freedom, privacy, and the future.

"We believed then that technology could change the world, that cryptography could liberate humanity," he murmurs to himself. "But we were wrong."

The old man's voice drops, tinged with bitterness:

"Technology did change the world, but it did not change human nature. Greed, fear, desire — these most primal emotions still drive people's behavior. Cryptography can provide tools, but it cannot provide morality. We can create decentralized systems, but we cannot create decentralized souls."

Outside the window, a dull thunderclap detonates in the distance.

"This is the paradox of the crypto realm," he continues writing. "It is both a tool of freedom and a breeding ground for greed; both a cradle of innovation and a casino of speculation; it connects the entire world, yet divides every individual."

### II. The Specter of Satoshi Nakamoto

Zhou Shiming's thoughts turn to the most mysterious figure — Satoshi Nakamoto.

Sixteen years. After posting his final message in December 2010, Satoshi vanished completely. No explanation, no farewell — he simply faded silently into the mists of the internet.

He (or she, or they) — the one million Bitcoin held as early mining rewards — still lies dormant on the blockchain, with no transfer records whatsoever. At current prices, these coins are worth over sixty billion dollars. For anyone who knows the private keys, moving them would take mere seconds. But Satoshi chose silence.

"Why?" Zhou Shiming asks himself countless times in his heart. "Why abandon this enormous fortune? Why pass up the chance to become a billionaire?"

He proposes several possible explanations:

Perhaps Satoshi is dead. Perhaps that mysterious figure had already passed in 2010, taking the secret of the private keys to the grave. Perhaps those coins will sleep forever in a digital tomb, becoming an eternal enigma on the blockchain.

Perhaps Satoshi lost the private keys. In those early days, Bitcoin was virtually worthless, and many people failed to properly safeguard their private keys. Perhaps Satoshi simply forgot where the private keys were stored, or the hard drive containing them had long since failed.

Perhaps Satoshi simply never needed the money, having accumulated vast wealth through other means long ago.

But Zhou Shiming believes there is another possibility — a more idealistic one:

Perhaps Satoshi deliberately left them untouched.

Perhaps Satoshi knew that if he moved those coins, if he revealed himself, if he attempted to control Bitcoin's direction of development, Bitcoin would transform from a decentralized currency into the creation of one person — a project that was centralized to some degree. His disappearance ensured that Bitcoin would truly become public property, free from any individual control.

"Satoshi created an organism, then let it grow freely," one Bitcoin developer once commented. "He did not塑造 himself as a leader or savior. He simply gave us the code, then retreated into the shadows. It was the greatest gift."

Zhou Shiming deeply agrees:

"Satoshi's disappearance is the most mysterious and significant event in crypto history. It created a vacuum, a power vacuum that no one could fill. In that vacuum, Bitcoin was able to develop according to its own logic, free from any individual will."

"In this sense, Satoshi never truly left. His specter still drifts through every corner of the crypto realm — in the hash of every block, in the synchronization of every node, in the faith of every holder."

The old man lifts his gaze, looking at the night sky beyond the window. Stars begin to twinkle, like countless eyes watching from the darkness.

"Are you satisfied, Satoshi?" he asks the void. "Is this the outcome you wanted?"

No answer comes. Only the wind howling outside the window, like some ancient echo in return.

### III. The Elegy of the Mining Dynasties

Zhou Shiming's thoughts turn to the builders of those mining empires.

Friedcat. Jiang Xinyu. That technical genius who dropped out of Yale, who shook the entire crypto world with his ASIC miners. His story is one of the most legendary and tragic chapters in crypto history.

Zhou Shiming still remembers Friedcat's first post on the Bitcointalk forum. It was the summer of 2012, Bitcoin hovering around ten dollars, most people still dismissing the project as a curious experiment. But Friedcat's post — announcing the development of ASIC miners — was like thunder in that tranquil community.

"We will produce the first truly large-scale ASIC miners," Friedcat wrote. "This will completely transform the landscape of Bitcoin mining."

He delivered. The ASICMiner ICO was a massive success, investors flooding in. Friedcat's team produced the first ASIC chips within mere months, their efficiency hundreds of times greater than the GPU miners of the day.

"That was the golden age of mining," Zhou Shiming recalls. "Friedcat's machines nearly monopolized global hashrate; he personally controlled over 40% of the Bitcoin network. It was the zenith of technical genius, the moment an empire was founded."

But after the peak, often comes the abyss.

In 2014, Friedcat vanished mysteriously. No warning, no explanation — he simply disappeared into the vast sea of humanity. Some said he met an untimely end at his Sichuan mining facility. Others claimed he fled overseas with enormous funds. Still others believed he was kidnapped, or even killed, by some mysterious organization.

The truth remains unknown to this day.

"Friedcat's disappearance is one of the greatest mysteries in crypto history," Zhou Shiming writes. "It was not merely a personal tragedy, but a turning point for the entire mining era."

After Friedcat vanished, his legacy was inherited and divided by others. Most notable among them was the rise of Bitmain.

Wu Jihan and Zhan Ketuan — these two names once shone brightest in the crypto world. The company they founded, Bitmain, became the world's largest ASIC miner manufacturer at its peak, commanding over 70% of market share. Their Antminer series spread across the globe, their mining pools controlled enormous hashrate, wealth accumulating at an astonishing rate.

"That's another empire's story," Zhou Shiming sighs. "A tale of ambition, betrayal, and destruction."

Bitmain's internal warfare was one of the bloodiest, most dramatic episodes of corporate politics in crypto history. Wu Jihan and Zhan Ketuan — these former comrades-in-arms — ultimately turned on each other, staging a jaw-dropping power struggle.

In October 2019, Wu Jihan struck suddenly at a late-night meeting, announcing the removal of Zhan Ketuan from all positions. Zhan was ousted, losing control of the company he had built with his own hands.

But Zhan Ketuan did not surrender. In June 2020, he hired security teams and forcibly reclaimed Bitmain's offices. The two sides clashed violently in the office; people were injured, some detained, police and ambulances screaming to the scene.

"It was a palace coup," Zhou Shiming recalls. "Bitmain's internal strife read like Shakespearean tragedy — both protagonists were brilliant, talented, ambitious men, but their greed and hunger for power ultimately destroyed everything."

The result of the internal war was Bitmain's fragmentation. The company was split in two, Wu Jihan and Zhan Ketuan each controlling different portions of the business, beginning a prolonged legal battle. Bitmain's IPO plans stalled, market share gradually eaten away by competitors.

"Friedcat and Bitmain's stories are emblematic of the mining era in crypto," Zhou Shiming concludes. "They demonstrate the power of technological innovation, yet also expose the fragility of human nature. In the face of enormous wealth, even the smartest, most talented individuals can lose their way and march toward destruction."

The old man pauses, his gaze growing distant:

"This is one of the crypto realm's deepest lessons. Technology can solve engineering problems, but it cannot solve human problems. We can create decentralized systems, but we cannot create decentralized hearts. Greed, envy, the lust for power — these emotions will ferment in any organization, no matter how decentralized or transparent it claims to be."

Outside the window, raindrops begin to fall, pattering against the glass. The old man rises, watching the rain trace paths down the pane.

"The age of mining is waning," he murmurs to himself. "ASIC hegemony, hashrate concentration, energy consumption — these were once the cornerstone of Bitcoin's security, yet now they have become its greatest controversies. What will the crypto realm of the future look like?"

### IV. The Border Between Darknet and Light

Zhou Shiming's thoughts drift to another dark corner — Silk Road, the underground marketplace where drugs were traded in Bitcoin.

Ross Ulbricht. That young man with the idealistic face, who claimed to be building a "free market," who was ultimately sentenced to life in prison as a criminal.

"The Silk Road story is one of the most complex and controversial in crypto history," Zhou Shiming writes. "It exposes Bitcoin's dark side, and provokes profound debates about freedom and regulation, privacy and security."

Ulbricht created Silk Road in 2011, leveraging Bitcoin's anonymity and the Tor network's concealment to establish an underground marketplace where almost anything could be traded. Drugs, forged documents, hacker services, weapons — if you could imagine an illegal commodity, you could find it on Silk Road.

"My original goal was to create a market without violence," Ulbricht later said in an interview. "In traditional drug trading, buyers and sellers face risks of violence, theft, and fraud. Silk Road provided a relatively safe, relatively fair platform where both parties could complete transactions without meeting face to face."

From a certain perspective, Ulbricht's argument was not entirely without merit. Silk Road did establish a reputation system, allowing buyers to rate sellers and reducing fraud. The platform also prohibited the sale of goods that "caused harm to others," such as child pornography and assassination services — though this "moral bottom line" appeared hollow and absurd to law enforcement.

But regardless, Silk Road's existence proved Bitcoin's practicality. Before Bitcoin, online illegal trading faced enormous payment obstacles — credit cards could be traced, bank transfers could be frozen, cash by mail was slow and unsafe. Bitcoin solved these problems, making online illegal trading more convenient than ever before.

"Silk Road gave Bitcoin its first genuine 'value,'" Zhou Shiming admits. "Not speculative value, but practical value. People were willing to buy Bitcoin to use the platform, willing to hold Bitcoin to complete transactions. This was a significant step for Bitcoin's transition from experiment to practical utility."

But the price of that step was enormous.

In October 2013, the FBI shut down Silk Road and arrested Ross Ulbricht. They captured him in a San Francisco library, where he was managing the site on his laptop. Police seized thousands of Bitcoin from the scene, worth over thirty million dollars at the time.

Ulbricht was ultimately sentenced to life in prison without parole. In the sentencing document, the judge wrote that Silk Road had caused countless drug addictions and deaths, and Ulbricht must pay the price for this.

"This sentence caused enormous controversy in the crypto world," Zhou Shiming recalls. "Many felt the sentence was excessive, that Ulbricht was merely a platform provider and should not bear full responsibility for users' actions. Others argued that Silk Road simply provided a marketplace — the real criminals were those selling drugs."

"But regardless, the Silk Road story left a profound imprint on the crypto realm. It proved that Bitcoin could be used for crime, that anonymous technology is a double-edged sword. From that point on, the label 'Bitcoin is a tool for criminals' has followed it to this day."

Zhou Shiming pauses:

"The lesson of Silk Road is that technology is neutral, but the use of technology has consequences. We can debate Ulbricht's moral responsibility, can debate the fairness of his sentence, but we cannot deny: Bitcoin did make illegal trading easier, did bring new challenges to law enforcement."

"This is why regulation was ultimately inevitable. Not because it opposes freedom, but because freedom must have boundaries. Absolute freedom means the strong exploiting the weak, means chaos and violence spreading unchecked. Digital currency must find balance between freedom and regulation — a difficult but necessary process."

Outside the window, the rain grows heavier, the wind fiercer. The old man watches the storm, his heart stirred by complex emotions.

"Silk Road is gone," he writes, "but similar platforms keep emerging endlessly. Darknet markets are like the Hydra — cut off one head, and two more grow. As long as people are willing to buy illegal goods, as long as people are willing to sell, this market will exist."

"Bitcoin simply made this market more efficient, more covert. It is not the root cause of the problem, but it is certainly part of the problem."

### V. The Rise and Fall of Exchanges — From Mt. Gox to FTX

Zhou Shiming's thoughts turn to another theme — exchanges, the most bustling yet dangerous stages in the crypto world.

Mt. Gox. Mark Karpeles. That portly, baby-faced young Frenchman, once the figure controlling the world's largest Bitcoin exchange.

"The Mt. Gox story is one of the most painful lessons in crypto history," Zhou Shiming writes. "It exposes the fragility of centralized exchanges, the cost of trust, how one person's arrogance can destroy countless lives."

Mt. Gox was founded in 2010, originally as a platform for trading Magic: The Gathering cards before transitioning to a Bitcoin exchange. At its peak during 2013–2014, it processed over 70% of global Bitcoin trading volume, making it the undisputed industry leader.

Karpeles took over Mt. Gox in 2011, transforming from an obscure programmer into one of the crypto world's most prominent figures. He lived lavishly in his Tokyo office, driving luxury cars, wearing designer clothing, presenting the airs of a tech mogul.

"But beneath that华丽外表," Zhou Shiming sighs, "lay a company riddled with management chaos, outdated technology, and gaping security holes."

In February 2014, Mt. Gox abruptly suspended withdrawals, then announced bankruptcy. The company claimed that approximately 850,000 Bitcoin — worth approximately $450 million at the time — had vanished due to hacking. This was the largest theft in crypto history, and one of the most destructive events.

"Mt. Gox's collapse triggered a multi-year 'crypto winter,'" Zhou Shiming recalls. "Bitcoin's price plummeted from $1,000 to below $200; countless investors lost everything; countless projects withered. The entire industry sank into deep crisis and doubt."

Karpeles was later arrested, charged with embezzlement and data manipulation. He spent months in a Japanese prison and was ultimately found guilty, though his sentence was relatively light. The missing Bitcoin — except for a small portion recovered — remains unaccounted for to this day.

"The lesson of Mt. Gox is profound," Zhou Shiming writes. "It teaches us that centralized exchanges are the most vulnerable link in the crypto realm. They control users' funds but lack adequate regulation and security measures. They are the primary targets of hacker attacks and the greatest temptation for internal malfeasance."

"But unfortunately, this lesson was not fully learned."

The old man's gaze shifts to another more recent case — FTX.

Sam Bankman-Fried (SBF). That self-proclaimed "effective altruist" prodigy trader, who built a multi-billion-dollar empire in three years, who was ultimately convicted of fraud and imprisoned.

"The FTX story is the most astonishing rise and fall in crypto history," Zhou Shiming writes. "It shows how the aura of 'genius' can conceal corruption, how the absence of regulation can amplify risk, how greed can take root in the most unexpected places."

SBF founded FTX in 2019, leveraging the quantitative trading skills he learned at Jane Street, rapidly building FTX into one of the world's largest cryptocurrency derivatives exchanges. His success story attracted countless investors — from retail to institutional, from sovereign wealth funds to pension funds, everyone wanted a piece of the action.

"SBF's smartest move was the image he crafted for himself," Zhou Shiming admits. "He wasn't the typical 'crypto bro' — T-shirt, flip-flops, crude language, and internet memes. He wore wrinkled suits, talked about 'effective altruism' and 'changing the world,' giving off the impression of an intellectual and idealist."

"This image earned him trust in traditional finance. While other crypto entrepreneurs were still struggling to open bank accounts, SBF was dining with senators, testifying before Congress, calling Wall Street titans his buddies."

But beneath that华丽 façade lay an elaborate scam.

Between FTX and its sister company, Alameda Research, existed opaque and unfair financial dealings. Alameda could use customer deposits for trading, could use FTX-issued tokens (FTT) as loan collateral, could access market information ahead of other users. All of this was concealed beneath complex accounting structures and false publicity.

In November 2022, FTX's lies were finally exposed. A competitor exchange's CEO questioned Alameda's financial status on Twitter (now X), triggering market panic. Users withdrew billions of dollars within days; FTX couldn't meet withdrawal demands and ultimately declared bankruptcy.

"SBF's fall was swift," Zhou Shiming writes. "From peak to bottom, in less than a week. When the truth emerged, people discovered that the 'genius' and 'philanthropist' persona was merely a carefully designed mask, concealing beneath it a greedy, arrogant, unscrupulous fraudster."

SBF was arrested in 2023 and charged with multiple fraud counts. During the trial, prosecutors presented overwhelming evidence that SBF had deliberately deceived investors, misappropriated customer funds, and manipulated markets. He was sentenced to twenty-five years in prison, becoming one of the most notorious criminals in crypto history.

"The damage from FTX's collapse was enormous," Zhou Shiming writes. "Millions of users lost their deposits, totaling billions of dollars. The entire cryptocurrency market fell into crisis again; Bitcoin's price plummeted; countless projects went bankrupt; the industry's reputation suffered severe damage."

"But more serious than the financial losses was the loss of trust. SBF betrayed so many people — his customers, his investors, his employees, even his romantic partner. He wrapped his greed in the cloak of 'effective altruism,' covered his fraud with the slogan of 'changing the world.' This betrayal was more destructive than any market crash."

The old man pauses, his gaze growing grim:

"Mt. Gox and FTX, eight years apart, tell similar stories. Both expose the dangers of centralized exchanges, show how human greed swells in environments lacking regulation, demonstrate how the aura of 'genius' causes people to lower their guard."

"We can build better technology, establish stricter rules, strengthen regulation and audits. But as long as human weakness exists, as long as greed and arrogance continue driving people's behavior, similar tragedies will recur."

"This is the crypto realm's fate — eternally oscillating between innovation and destruction, constantly struggling between ideals and reality."

## Chapter Two: Forces Rising

### I. Ethereum's Eternal Flame

Zhou Shiming's thoughts shift from the dark corners to the light — those forces bringing hope and innovation to the crypto world.

Vitalik Buterin. That frail, baby-faced Russian-Canadian youth, who founded Ethereum at nineteen, who remains to this day an idealist leader in the industry.

"Vitalik's story is one of the most inspiring in crypto history," Zhou Shiming writes. "It demonstrates the power of youth, the possibility of idealism, how technology can transcend speculation and genuinely change the world."

Vitalik published the Ethereum whitepaper in 2013, proposing an entirely new blockchain platform — capable not only of processing monetary transactions but of executing arbitrarily complex programs. This is the concept of the "smart contract": code is law, programs are contracts, executed automatically without intermediaries.

"When I first read Ethereum's whitepaper," Zhou Shiming recalls, "I knew it would be revolutionary. Bitcoin is digital gold, a store of value. But Ethereum is digital oil, a fuel that can drive countless applications."

Ethereum launched officially in 2015 and quickly attracted global developer attention. People began building all manner of applications on Ethereum — DeFi, NFT, DAO, supply chain management, identity verification, gaming, social networks...

"Ethereum inaugurated the blockchain 2.0 era," Zhou Shiming writes. "It proved that blockchain technology isn't limited to currency — it can be used to build entirely new digital worlds. In these worlds, code is the ultimate authority, trust is established through cryptography, and intermediaries are eliminated wherever possible."

But Ethereum's path was far from smooth.

In 2016, Ethereum faced its gravest crisis — The DAO hack. A decentralized autonomous organization called The DAO had raised over $150 million in Ether through crowdfunding, but shortly after launch it was attacked by a hacker, losing over a third of its funds.

"That was a life-or-death test for Ethereum," Zhou Shiming recalls. "The community faced a difficult choice: persist with 'code is law' and let the hacker keep the funds, or intervene, roll back the transactions, and recover the losses?"

Vitalik and his team chose the latter. They promoted a hard fork, creating a new blockchain, rolling back the hacker's transactions, recovering most of the stolen funds. Those who opposed this intervention continued maintaining the original chain, forming Ethereum Classic (ETC).

"This decision caused enormous controversy in the crypto world," Zhou Shiming admits. "Some felt Vitalik betrayed blockchain's core principles — once code is deployed, it shouldn't be changed. But others argued that facing such a massive hack, intervention was necessary, that it protected users' interests."

"Regardless, Ethereum survived the crisis. It proved that blockchain projects can adapt and evolve, can find balance between principle and pragmatic response."

Since then, Ethereum has undergone numerous major upgrades — transitioning from Proof of Work to Proof of Stake, scaling from Layer 1 to Layer 2, evolving from a single chain to a multi-chain ecosystem. Each upgrade came with controversy and challenges, but Ethereum has remained the leader in smart contract platforms.

"What distinguishes Vitalik is his persistent idealism," Zhou Shiming writes. "While other founders busied themselves cashing out and splurging, he continued focusing on technical research, pushing Ethereum's development, contemplating blockchain technology's social impact."

"He's not without flaws. He can be overly idealistic at times, lacking business acumen, sometimes indecisive in decision-making. But these 'flaws' are precisely what make him respectable — he's not the kind of entrepreneur who pursues profit at any cost, but someone who genuinely believes technology can change the world."

The old man gazes at Ethereum's price on the screen — three thousand five hundred dollars. Though down from its all-time high, Ethereum's ecosystem remains vibrant, still the preferred platform for most DeFi and NFT projects.

"Ethereum's success isn't merely one project's success — it's the success of an idea. The idea of smart contracts, of decentralized applications, of programmable money. These ideas will continue influencing the crypto world's future, even if Ethereum itself is eventually supplanted by other platforms."

### II. Sovereign Nations' Embrace

Zhou Shiming's thoughts turn to another significant development — sovereign nations' embrace of cryptocurrency.

El Salvador. Nayib Bukele. That young, baseball-cap-loving president who made the astonishing move in 2021 of making Bitcoin legal tender.

"El Salvador's Bitcoin experiment is a milestone in cryptocurrency history," Zhou Shiming writes. "It was the first time a sovereign nation formally recognized Bitcoin's legal status, incorporating it into the national monetary system."

Bukele's decision caused enormous excitement in the crypto world. Supporters viewed it as a major step toward Bitcoin mainstream adoption, a bold challenge to the traditional financial system, a daring attempt to provide financial services to the unbanked. Critics called it a dangerous gamble, irresponsible behavior placing a national economy at the mercy of a highly volatile asset.

"Whatever one's views," Zhou Shiming admits, "El Salvador's experiment provided invaluable case studies for cryptocurrency. It demonstrated both the opportunities and challenges of implementing cryptocurrency in a developing nation, the complex interplay of technology, politics, and economics."

The results were mixed. On one hand, El Salvador did attract significant international attention and investment; tourism grew; some unbanked citizens did gain access to financial services through Bitcoin wallets. On the other hand, Bitcoin's wild price swings put enormous pressure on national finances; institutions like the International Monetary Fund (IMF) and World Bank downgraded El Salvador's ratings; international investors' confidence in the country declined.

"El Salvador's lesson is this," Zhou Shiming writes. "Making Bitcoin legal tender is an extremely complex and risky decision. It requires robust technical infrastructure, widespread public education, sound regulatory frameworks, and adequate risk management. Simply declaring Bitcoin legal tender does not automatically produce the intended effects."

But El Salvador was not the only nation interested in cryptocurrency.

The United States — the world's largest economy — made another historic decision in 2024: approving Bitcoin exchange-traded funds (ETFs) for listing. This meant ordinary investors could invest in Bitcoin through traditional brokerage accounts without directly purchasing and holding cryptocurrency.

"ETF approval was a crucial step toward crypto mainstream adoption," Zhou Shiming writes. "It provided compliant, convenient investment channels for institutional investors, brought billions of dollars in new capital to Bitcoin, and offered powerful endorsement of cryptocurrency's legitimacy and credibility."

Following ETF approval, Bitcoin hit new all-time highs, breaking seventy thousand dollars. Wall Street giants — BlackRock, Fidelity, Grayscale — rushed to launch their own Bitcoin ETF products, with intensely competitive dynamics.

"This is a new era for the crypto realm," Zhou Shiming sighs. "From the margins to the mainstream, from underground to above ground, from rebellion to embrace. Bitcoin is no longer a tool for 'criminals' or 'geeks,' but an asset class recognized by mainstream financial institutions."

But Zhou Shiming remains cautious:

"ETF approval doesn't mean risks have disappeared. Conversely, it may have introduced new risks — centralization, regulatory interference, market manipulation. When Bitcoin was incorporated into the traditional financial system, it inevitably became subject to that system's influences and constraints."

"This is the crypto realm's eternal paradox: the more mainstream, the safer, but also the more it loses its original spirit and freedom."

### III. Halving and Rebirth

Zhou Shiming's thoughts turn to one of Bitcoin's unique mechanisms — Halving.

The Bitcoin protocol stipulates that every 210,000 blocks (approximately every four years), the miner's block reward is halved. This means the rate of new Bitcoin issuance gradually decreases, until ultimately reaching the ceiling of 21 million coins.

"Halving is one of Bitcoin's core economic mechanisms," Zhou Shiming writes. "It ensures Bitcoin's scarcity, creates gold-like properties, and provides a predictable pattern for Bitcoin's price cycles."

Historically, every halving has been accompanied by Bitcoin's price increase. After the first halving in 2012, Bitcoin rose from $12 to over $1,000. After the second halving in 2016, Bitcoin climbed from $650 to nearly $20,000. After the third halving in 2020, Bitcoin surged from $8,000 to over $60,000.

"Halving's effects are complex," Zhou Shiming explains. "On one hand, it reduces new coin supply, pushing prices higher when demand remains constant. On the other hand, it attracts media attention and speculator influx, forming a self-fulfilling prophecy."

In April 2024, Bitcoin experienced its fourth halving. The block reward fell from 6.25 Bitcoin to 3.125, and the daily issuance of new coins dropped from approximately 900 to approximately 450.

"The fourth halving was different from its predecessors," Zhou Shiming writes. "This time, Bitcoin had ETF support, institutional investor participation, and more mature market infrastructure. Halving was no longer an internal crypto event, but a matter closely watched by global financial markets."

Post-halving market performance did not disappoint supporters. Bitcoin's price continued climbing after the halving; though volatility remained, the overall trend pointed upward.

"Halving is Bitcoin's 'heartbeat,'" Zhou Shiming draws an analogy. "Every four years, it reminds people of Bitcoin's scarcity and value proposition. It is a natural cycle, an immutable rule, a mechanism controlled by no one."

"In this sense, halving embodies Bitcoin's most core spirit — rules supreme, code is law, free from human intervention. Whether you like it or not, however the market fluctuates, halving happens on schedule, as unchangeable as Earth's orbit."

The old man gazes at Bitcoin's price curve on the screen — that curve extending from 2009 to the present, having experienced countless crashes and recoveries, yet always maintaining an upward trend.

"Halving is a symbol of the crypto realm's cycle," he writes. "Each halving is a death and rebirth, a liquidation and reconstruction, a culling of the weak and a reward for the strong. It embodies the brutal laws of market economics, as well as life's own cyclical规律."

"The tempest never ends, the cycle never ceases. This is Bitcoin's destiny, and the crypto realm's destiny."

## Chapter Three: The Race for the Future

### I. Web3's Utopia and the Chasm of Reality

Zhou Shiming's thoughts shift from the past to the future — those new trends and forces shaping the crypto world's fate.

Web3. This concept, which gained popularity in the early 2020s, promises an entirely new internet — decentralized, user-owned, blockchain-based.

"Web3's vision is seductive," Zhou Shiming writes. "It promises to liberate the internet from the control of a few tech giants, to let users truly own their data and identity, to allow value to flow freely across the internet, to let creators earn directly from fans without platform intermediaries taking a cut."

In Web3's vision, social media would no longer be controlled by Facebook or Twitter but managed by DAOs. Users would own their social graphs, freely migrating them from one platform to another. Content creators would sell works directly to fans through NFTs, no longer at the mercy of video platforms' (YouTube) or music platforms' (Spotify) algorithms and policies. Online identity would no longer rely on Google or Facebook login systems but would be represented by user-controlled encrypted wallets.

"It's a beautiful vision," Zhou Shiming admits. "It carries forward the ideals of the cypherpunk movement, echoes Bitcoin's original spirit, and opens new domains for blockchain technology applications."

But between vision and reality lies an enormous chasm.

"Web3's greatest challenge is user experience," Zhou Shiming points out. "Current Web3 applications remain too complex and obscure for most ordinary users. Managing private keys, understanding gas fees, coping with network congestion — these technical barriers shut out the majority of users."

"Furthermore, Web3's decentralization promises are often compromised in practice. Many so-called 'decentralized' applications actually still rely on centralized infrastructure — servers, DNS, browser extensions. DAO governance is often controlled by a few whales, and so-called 'community decisions' are merely formal democracy."

The rise and fall of NFTs is a prime example.

In 2021, NFTs became the hottest concept in the crypto world. Art, music, gaming items, virtual land — everything could be tokenized and traded on the blockchain. Some NFT works sold for millions of dollars, attracting countless celebrities, brands, and investors.

"The NFT frenzy was one of the most spectacular bubbles in crypto history," Zhou Shiming writes. "It demonstrated humanity's obsession with scarcity and ownership, and how speculative psychology can drive any new concept to wild extremes."

But bubbles inevitably burst. During 2022–2023, the NFT market experienced a severe collapse. Most NFT project values plummeted over 90%; many projects were revealed as scams or air projects; countless investors lost everything.

"The NFT lesson is this," Zhou Shiming concludes. "Technology itself cannot create value. NFTs are a tool — they can be used to create genuine value (such as artists' copyright protection, in-game item ownership), or they can be used for speculation and fraud. The key lies in how the tool is used, not in the tool itself."

"Web3's future depends on whether it can genuinely solve real problems, whether it can provide better user experience than existing solutions, whether it can establish sustainable business models. If these conditions aren't met, Web3 may remain a beautiful utopia, forever stalled at the conceptual stage."

---

### II. The Shadow of Central Bank Digital Currencies

Zhou Shiming's thoughts turn to another trend that could profoundly impact the crypto world — Central Bank Digital Currencies (CBDCs).

China — the world's most populous nation and second-largest economy — has led the world in digital currency. The digital yuan (e-CNY) has been piloted in multiple cities, with cumulative transaction volumes exceeding hundreds of billions of yuan.

"Central Bank Digital Currencies are fundamentally different from Bitcoin and cryptocurrency," Zhou Shiming writes. "CBDCs are digital forms of fiat currency issued and controlled by central banks, retaining the centralized attributes of traditional currency while merely changing the technological form."

CBDC proponents argue they offer many benefits: reducing cash management costs, improving payment efficiency, enhancing monetary policy transmission, combating money laundering and tax evasion, and providing financial services to the unbanked.

But critics — especially the cryptocurrency community — hold strong skepticism toward CBDCs.

"The greatest threat of CBDCs lies in control," Zhou Shiming writes. "Unlike cash and decentralized cryptocurrency, every CBDC transaction can be tracked and monitored by the central bank. This means governments would gain unprecedented financial surveillance capabilities, able to scrutinize every citizen's spending."

"More terrifyingly, CBDCs could be used to implement 'programmable money' — governments could set conditions on currency use, such as restricting purchases of certain goods, imposing time limits on consumption, or even directly freezing funds upon detecting 'suspicious' activity."

The Chinese government has already demonstrated such capabilities in e-CNY pilots. In certain pilot scenarios, the digital yuan was designed to be usable only at specific merchants or only during specific time periods. This "programmability" may serve policy objectives, but it poses an enormous threat to personal liberty.

"The contest between CBDCs and cryptocurrency," Zhou Shiming writes, "is essentially a competition between two vastly different financial philosophies. On one side: centralization, control, surveillance; on the other: decentralization, freedom, privacy. These two forces will continue grappling for decades, determining the final form of digital currency."

"For the crypto world, CBDCs are both threat and opportunity. The threat: if CBDCs gain widespread adoption, they may squeeze cryptocurrency's living space, especially in payments and daily transactions. The opportunity: CBDC introduction may familiarize more people with digital currency concepts, thereby paving the way for cryptocurrency's adoption."

Zhou Shiming pauses:

"In the end, the choice belongs to the public," he writes. "People must choose between convenience and freedom, between surveillance and privacy, between centralization and decentralization. This choice will determine our financial future, and will also determine our social form."

---

### III. The Fusion of AI and Blockchain

Zhou Shiming's thoughts turn to another technology that could reshape the crypto world's future — Artificial Intelligence (AI).

"The fusion of AI and blockchain is one of the most compelling trends in recent years," Zhou Shiming writes. "These two technologies challenge existing power structures from different angles: AI challenges humanity's cognitive monopoly, blockchain challenges humanity's financial monopoly. Their combination may produce powerful synergies."

Current AI-blockchain integration manifests primarily in the following areas:

First, AI is used to optimize blockchain performance. Machine learning algorithms can predict network congestion, optimize transaction fee settings, and detect anomalous transactions and fraud. These applications enhance blockchain efficiency and security.

Second, blockchain is used to address problems in the AI domain. For example, blockchain can verify AI model training data, ensuring data authenticity and traceability. Blockchain can also be used for decentralized AI model governance, allowing communities to participate in model development and decision-making.

Third, new concepts like "AI Agents" are emerging. These are AI-driven autonomous programs that can execute complex tasks on the blockchain — trading, investing, governance, content creation — without direct human intervention.

"AI agents may represent the next evolutionary stage of the crypto realm," Zhou Shiming writes. "Imagine a DAO managed entirely by AI, automatically adjusting strategies based on market conditions, automatically executing decisions based on community votes, running 24/7 without being affected by human emotions."

"Of course, this also brings new risks and challenges. If an AI agent makes wrong decisions, who bears responsibility? If an AI agent is hacked or manipulated, what are the consequences? If AI agents conflict with each other, how is coordination achieved?"

"The answers to these questions remain unclear. But one thing is certain: the fusion of AI and blockchain will open entirely new frontiers, bringing unprecedented opportunities and challenges."

## Chapter Four: The Eternal Return

### I. The Specter of Satoshi Nakamoto

Zhou Shiming's thoughts return once more to the most mysterious figure — Satoshi Nakamoto.

Sixteen years. Those one million Bitcoin still slumber on the blockchain, untouched. Satoshi's specter drifts through every corner of the crypto world, an eternal enigma and symbol.

"Satoshi's specter has multiple meanings," Zhou Shiming writes. "First, it is a symbol of anonymity. In an increasingly transparent world, Satoshi proved that anonymity remains possible — not merely technically possible, but socially and politically possible."

"Second, it is the art of disappearance. Satoshi chose to depart when Bitcoin needed him most — an act of supreme wisdom. Founders often become bottlenecks and single points of failure for projects; their presence can impede organic community development. Satoshi's disappearance allowed Bitcoin to transcend its creator and become a truly decentralized project."

"Third, it is a symbol of wealth — or rather, of not-wealth. That $60 billion in assets deliberately left untouched is unprecedented in human history. It proves that some things matter more than money — principles, ideals, conviction."

Zhou Shiming pauses:

"But I prefer to understand Satoshi's specter from another angle," he continues. "It is not one person's specter, but a specter of an era, a spirit's specter."

"Satoshi represents a particular zeitgeist — distrust of the existing financial system, pursuit of decentralization ideals, faith in cryptographic technology, longing for personal freedom. This spirit was born in the 2008 financial crisis, expressed in Bitcoin's whitepaper, and continues through the entire development of the crypto realm."

"Whenever a new blockchain project launches, whenever a developer commits code, whenever a user downloads a wallet, Satoshi's spirit is in some sense perpetuated. He (she, they) may never appear, but his creation has gained its own life."

Outside the window, the rain has stopped, but the wind still howls. The old man gazes at the night sky beyond the window, stars glimpsed between parting clouds.

"Satoshi's specter is also a warning," Zhou Shiming writes. "It reminds us that technology is created by people, but technological development often exceeds creators' control. Bitcoin has evolved into something Satoshi may never have anticipated — attracting speculators, provoking regulatory wars, consuming enormous energy, creating countless fortunes and tragedies."

"This is technology's fate. Once released into the world, it develops according to its own logic, absorbs every available resource, adapts to every environment, regardless of the creator's original intentions."

---

### II. The Eternal Cycle

Zhou Shiming's thoughts turn to a deeper theme — the cycle.

"The history of the crypto realm is a history of cycles," he writes. "Bull markets and bear markets alternate; bubbles and crashes cycle; hope and despair rotate. Each cycle brings new participants, new stories, new lessons, but the underlying pattern remains constant."

"Mt. Gox collapsed in 2011, the second crash came in 2014, the crypto winter of 2018, the FTX crisis in 2022 — different names, different causes each time, but the underlying pattern is the same: greed leads to excessive leverage, excessive leverage leads to fragility, fragility leads to collapse."

"Similarly, each recovery follows a similar pattern: hope is born in despair, energy accumulates in downturns, new technology or events trigger new cycles."

"This cycle is not accidental — it is a reflection of human nature. Greed and fear are humanity's most primal emotions; they are amplified in markets, spread through crowds, forming cyclical fluctuations. As long as human nature remains unchanged, the market's cycles will continue."

But Zhou Shiming also perceives a deeper cycle — the cycle of technology, the cycle of civilization, the cycle of the cosmos.

"From a grander temporal scale," he writes, "the crypto realm is merely one episode in humanity's technological history. Before it came the rise of the internet, the invention of telegraph and telephone, the revolution of printing. Each technology was once viewed as a disruptive force, once provoked similar manias and panics, and ultimately merged into society's mainstream."

"On an even grander scale," Zhou Shiming's gaze grows vast, "what we may witness is civilization itself cycling. Humanity moved from hunting and gathering to agriculture, from agriculture to industry, from industry to the information age. Each transition brought enormous social change, redefining the structures of power, wealth, and meaning."

"Digital currency may represent the dawn of the next transition — from the information age to an age of value, from centralization to decentralization, from national monopoly to global consensus."

"But this is mere speculation. The future is unpredictable, just as no one in 2008 could have predicted Bitcoin's today. All we can do is observe, participate, record, and find our place in history's long river."

---

### III. The Tempest Never Ends

Zhou Shiming rises and walks to the window, gazing at the nocturnal scenery beyond.

Hong Kong's night remains resplendent, ten thousand lights like stars scattered across the earth. This city has weathered countless storms — colonization and reunion, prosperity and crisis, turbulence and tranquility — yet it still stands here, witnessing history's changes.

"The tempest never ends," he murmurs. "The realm endures forever."

This is his ultimate judgment on the crypto world.

"The crypto realm will not disappear," he writes. "It may change form, may experience more crashes and reconstructions, may face stricter regulation and challenges, but it will not disappear. Because it is rooted in humanity's deepest longings — the longing for freedom, the pursuit of wealth, the passion for innovation, the hope for the future."

"As long as these longings exist, the crypto realm will exist in some form. It may no longer look as it does today, may no longer be 'cryptocurrency,' 'blockchain,' 'Web3' — these specific concepts — but the underlying spirit will endure."

"This is the meaning of the tempest. The wind rises from beneath the duckweed; the clouds gather at the horizon's edge. No one can predict what happens in the next moment, no one knows where the next legend or tragedy will unfold."

"But this is precisely the crypto realm's charm. In a world of certainty, it offers uncertainty; in rigid systems, it offers possibility; in mediocre lives, it offers adventure."

"For those who choose to enter this realm," Zhou Shiming writes finally, "I have some advice:"

"Maintain a clear mind. Stay calm in the face of greed and fear, stay rational amid euphoria and despair. Don't let FOMO drive impulsive decisions, don't let FUD shake your conviction."

"Do your own research. Trust no one's promises, chase no 'guaranteed return' projects. Remember: if something sounds too good to be true, it probably isn't."

"Only invest money you can afford to lose. The crypto realm is a high-risk market; any investment could result in total loss. Don't speculate with money you can't afford to lose, don't borrow to invest, don't wager your entire net worth on a single project."

"Remember the original vision. Digital currency is not merely a speculative tool — it is a movement about freedom, privacy, and financial inclusion. Don't let greed blind your eyes to why we began this journey."

"Finally, enjoy the tempest. Win or lose, this is a rare era, an era brimming with transformation and opportunity. Being able to witness and participate in this historic change is itself a kind of fortune."

Outside the window, the wind rises, dark clouds gathering on the horizon, obscuring the moon. A new storm is brewing.

Zhou Shiming closes his laptop and steps onto the balcony. Sea wind brushes through his white hair, carrying a briny dampness. He gazes at those gathering clouds, a faint smile curving his lips.

"Come then," he says softly. "Let the storm rage more violently."

"The tempest never ends. The realm endures forever. And now, let us hear the next legend unfold."

## Epilogue: To Be Continued

It was the last day of 2024.

Old man Zhou Shiming spent his ninety-third birthday in his old house on The Peak. His body had weakened considerably, but those eyes still shone bright, still flickered with curiosity and passion for the world.

Family and friends surrounded him, celebrating his birthday. Candles on the cake flickered with warm light, illuminating his lined face.

"Grandfather," his granddaughter asked, "you've witnessed the crypto realm's entire history — what do you think its future holds?"

The old man smiled, gazing at Victoria Harbour beyond the window. Fireworks were blooming in the night sky, illuminating this city that never sleeps.

"The future?" he repeated, his voice hoarse but firm. "The future is unpredictable, child. No one knows whether Bitcoin will reach a million dollars or go to zero, no one knows whether Ethereum will be supplanted or continue flourishing, no one knows when or where the next Satoshi will appear."

"But I know one thing: change will not stop. Technology will continue developing, society will keep evolving, humanity will keep searching for better ways to organize their lives."

"The crypto realm is merely part of this larger transformation. It may succeed, may fail, may be supplanted by other technologies. But the underlying spirit — the pursuit of freedom, the passion for innovation, the hope for a better world — these things will never disappear."

He paused, his gaze growing profound:

"In this life, I have witnessed too many storms and changes. I saw cryptography born, saw Bitcoin published, watched countless projects rise and fall. I saw greed destroy people, and ideals inspire people. I saw technology's power, and human nature's fragility."

"If there is one thing I most want to tell you, it is this: stay curious, stay humble, stay kind."

"Curiosity keeps you exploring new possibilities; humility keeps you aware of your limitations; kindness keeps you caring for others even as you pursue success."

"The crypto realm is full of opportunities, but also full of traps. Only those with both wisdom and virtue can survive and thrive here for the long term."

Outside the window, the New Year's bells began to toll. 2025 was approaching, bringing new hope, new challenges, new tempests.

The old man closed his eyes, a satisfied smile on his face.

"This life of mine has no regrets," he said softly. "I witnessed an era, recorded an era, and contributed my strength to that era."

"Now, it is your turn."

"Go and write the next legend."

Outside the window, fireworks bloomed in the night sky, like countless new possibilities shining in the darkness. The wind rose, bringing new airs, new stories, new cycles.

The tempest never ends.

The realm endures forever.
