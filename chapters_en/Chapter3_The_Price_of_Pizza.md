## Prologue: The Hum of Mining Rigs and the Restless Dawn

### I. The Digital Wilderness of 2010

Spring 2010. In an ordinary suburban home in Jacksonville, Florida, Laszlo Hanyecz stared at the flickering numbers on his computer screen, the whir of fans spinning at high speed filling his ears. The sound was like the buzzing of insects' wings, or the distant roar of a beehive, piercing the silence of the late night. The room carried a distinctive smell — overheated electronics, bitter coffee, and a hint of sweat mingled together. This was the scent of a digital miner.

His study had been converted into a "mine" — though no one used that word yet back then. Six computers sat side by side on the floor, their cases open, revealing intricate wiring and blinking indicator lights. The graphics cards ran at full capacity, radiating fierce heat that raised the room's temperature at least ten degrees above the outside air. Laszlo wore a tank top and shorts, fine beads of sweat on his forehead, but his eyes never left the screen.

The numbers on the screen kept scrolling — the interface of the mining software he was running. Green text scrolled across the black background, displaying block height, hash rate, and the number of connected nodes. To ordinary people, these numbers meant nothing. But to Laszlo, they were a cipher leading to the future.

"Block 127," he murmured to himself, voice hoarse. "Mined another two thousand coins today."

He lifted the cup of coffee on his desk — long gone cold — and took a sip. The bitter taste spread across his tongue, but he'd grown used to it. Over the past three months, his life rhythm had been completely disrupted — coding at his company during the day, then coming home and coding some more, except this time for himself. Sleep had become a luxury. He averaged just four hours a night, the rest of his time spent mining and studying Bitcoin.

Laszlo was an ordinary programmer, a software development engineer at a local tech company. He was twenty-eight, married, with two young children. To outsiders, his life trajectory was unremarkable: graduated from college, entered the IT industry, got married and had kids, bought a house and a car, paid the mortgage on time every month, and took his family to the park for picnics on weekends. He was the kind of person who disappeared into a crowd — average height, brown hair, black-framed glasses, a somewhat shy smile.

But on this spring night, Laszlo felt he was part of something grand. This feeling reminded him of the first time he'd encountered the internet, the first time he'd written a successful program, the first time he'd understood the spirit of open source. That excitement, that anticipation, that possibility of changing the world — it made his heart race.

He first heard about Bitcoin at the end of 2009. Back then, he'd seen a post on a tech forum about a "decentralized digital currency." The post mentioned that someone had created an electronic cash system that needed no banks, no governments, no intermediaries. Laszlo initially thought it was another idealist's pipe dream — he'd seen too many similar attempts, from eCash to DigiGold, none of which had truly succeeded.

But when he downloaded the Bitcoin software and studied the source code, he realized this time was different.

"It's brilliant," he told his wife at the time, eyes shining. "Blockchain, proof of work, the longest chain rule — Satoshi solved a problem none of us could crack. This could change everything."

His wife simply smiled and nodded. She was used to her husband's "crazy ideas" — from Linux to open source software, from cryptography to privacy protection, Laszlo was always passionate about the cutting edge of technology. She didn't really understand what he was talking about, but she supported his hobby as long as it didn't affect the family's finances.

Laszlo began mining Bitcoin. At first, he just used his laptop, running the mining software in his spare time. But soon, he found the competition intensifying — more and more miners were joining the network, and block difficulty kept climbing. He needed more hashing power.

So he bought additional graphics cards and assembled dedicated mining machines. He spent several thousand dollars — no small sum for a middle-class family — but he believed it was a worthwhile investment. Not for making money, at least not yet. He believed in Bitcoin's vision, believed in a decentralized future, believed that technology could change the world.

By May 2010, Laszlo had mined over 70,000 Bitcoin. These digital assets sat in his digital wallet, with no market price, no exchange to trade on, no merchants willing to accept them. They were just encrypted strings, proving that he was maintaining a network, participating in an experiment, believing in a dream.

"How much are these coins worth?" his friends asked him.

Laszlo shrugged. "Right now? Pretty much nothing. But in the future — who knows?"

What he didn't know was that his fate was about to become inextricably linked with two pizzas, becoming the most famous and poignant legend in crypto history.

### II. The Hustle and Bustle of the Bitcointalk Forum

Meanwhile, on the other side of the network, the Bitcoin community was growing rapidly. This was a world without borders, a digital nation built on code and ideals. Here, time zones lost their meaning, skin color became irrelevant, and the only thing that mattered was your understanding of technology and your pursuit of freedom.

Bitcointalk.org was the heart of this community. This forum, created by Satoshi Nakamoto, had attracted thousands of registered users from around the world — the United States, Europe, Asia, South America. Among them were cryptographers, programmers, libertarians, anarchists, speculators, and idealists. They exchanged technology, shared ideas, and debated the future in this virtual space.

The forum's atmosphere was idealistic, even utopian. Most people believed Bitcoin was not just a new currency, but a revolution — a rebellion against the banking system, a challenge to government control, a pursuit of individual freedom. Every post was filled with passion and conviction, every discussion like a battle fought for the future.

"We are making history," one user wrote in a post. "When our grandchildren ask about the origins of digital currency, we can tell them that we were here, on this forum, witnessing it all. Our code will change the world's financial landscape, and our conviction will ignite the flame of liberty."

But alongside the idealism, there were also pragmatic discussions. As the number of miners increased, so did Bitcoin's production. People began asking a critical question: what are these coins actually for? This was the first step from idealism to pragmatism, a turning point from theory to practice.

In the very earliest days, Bitcoin was merely a toy for geeks. Miners sent Bitcoin to each other to test the system, like children passing glass marbles back and forth. But these coins had no "real" value — you couldn't buy coffee with them, couldn't pay rent, couldn't pay bills. They were ghosts of the digital world, floating at the edge of the real economy, waiting for the moment when they would be given meaning.

Some users tried to give Bitcoin real value. In October 2009, a user operating under the pseudonym "NewLibertyStandard" proposed the first Bitcoin exchange rate. Based on the electricity cost required to mine Bitcoin, he calculated 1 BTC = $0.000994. This price was based on production cost, not market supply and demand — because there was no market yet.

"If we can establish an exchange rate," NewLibertyStandard wrote, "people will know the value of Bitcoin and can begin trading with it. This is the first step toward making Bitcoin a real currency. We need to prove that Bitcoin is not just a technical experiment — it can become a medium of exchange, a store of value."

But most people were skeptical. Who would be willing to accept a digital currency with no government backing, no physical support, and wild price swings? Who would trade their goods or services for these intangible digital tokens? It was an age-old question — which came first, the chicken or the egg? For Bitcoin to have value, someone had to be willing to accept it; but for people to accept it, it had to have value first.

Bitcoin needed a breakthrough, a moment proving it could be used in the real world. This breakthrough would begin on an unremarkable afternoon, from a seemingly absurd request.

### III. The Aroma of Pizza

May 18, 2010. Tuesday. 3:17 PM.

The craving was so intense that he couldn't focus on his code. He stood up, walked to the kitchen, opened the fridge — only to find it completely empty. His wife had taken the kids to the supermarket and wouldn't be back for hours. He could use his credit card to order pizza online, but that was too ordinary, too boring.

Suddenly, an idea struck him. Why not pay with Bitcoin? This was a perfect opportunity to demonstrate Bitcoin's practicality, to show that digital currency could be used in the real world. If he could buy pizza with Bitcoin, it would be a historic moment.

He returned to his computer, opened Bitcointalk, and typed words that would go down in history:

"I'll pay 10,000 Bitcoin for a few pizzas, like two large ones, so I'll have some left over for tomorrow. I like ones with onions, peppers, sausage, mushrooms, etc. — just standard toppings, nothing weird like fish or anything similar. If you're interested, let me know and we can arrange the transaction."

He paused, considering whether to add some explanation. Then he added one more sentence:

"I just think it would be fun to pay with Bitcoin if someone could bring me pizza or help me order delivery."

He took a deep breath and clicked "Post." At that moment, he had no idea that his fate had been rewritten, that this simple request would become the most famous story in crypto history, that these two pizzas would ultimately be worth hundreds of millions of dollars.

The post attracted some attention on the forum, but the initial responses were confusion and mockery.

"10,000 Bitcoin?" one user replied. "Are you insane? These coins are worthless! You should be holding them right now, waiting for the price to rise."

"I think it's a great idea," another user wrote. "If someone is willing to accept Bitcoin for pizza, that proves Bitcoin can function as a medium of exchange. This is an important step in ecosystem development."

"The question is, who would be willing to accept it?" a third person asked. "Who would trade a real pizza for these digital tokens? This is insane."

Days passed with no response to Laszlo's request. He began to doubt his judgment, wondering if the idea was too far ahead of its time. He almost forgot about it, continuing with his mining and coding. But that request, like a seed, lay quietly in a corner of the forum, waiting for the moment to sprout.

### IV. The Shadow of Mt. Gox

On another end of the Bitcoin community, a young man was hatching a completely different plan. This plan would change Bitcoin's history, bring unprecedented liquidity to digital currency, and sow the seeds of future catastrophe.

Mark Karpeles sat in a cramped apartment in Tokyo's Shibuya district, staring at his laptop. Outside the window was Tokyo's bustling nightscape — neon lights flashing, crowds flowing like rivers. At the Shibuya Crossing, the world's busiest pedestrian intersection, thousands of people穿梭其中 every minute. But for Karpeles, the outside world seemed not to exist. He was immersed in the world of code, line after line of Java code flowing across the screen like the pulse of the digital world.

The room was a mess — empty takeout boxes, energy drink cans, and tech books scattered everywhere. Anime posters and code flowcharts were pinned to the walls. This was a typical programmer's apartment, a creator's nest.

Karpeles was twenty-four years old, French, but had been living in Japan for several years. He was a technical prodigy who had been coding since childhood; by sixteen, he was already an experienced developer. But he never fit in at traditional workplaces — he disliked the nine-to-five life, resented being given orders, hated office politics. He craved freedom, craved creation, craved control over his own destiny.

He had come to Japan because he loved the culture — anime, gaming, technology, order. Japan was a paradise for tech enthusiasts, the frontier of digital innovation. He found work at a tech company in Tokyo, but quickly grew bored. In early 2010, he quit his job and decided to start his own business.

Karpeles first heard about Bitcoin at the end of 2009. At the time, he was researching a project called "Magic: The Gathering Online eXchange" — a platform for trading virtual gaming cards. The platform never truly took off, but in the process of researching it, he encountered the concept of Bitcoin.

"This is interesting," he thought at the time, eyes fixed on the whitepaper on his screen. "A completely decentralized currency system. No central bank, no government control, no intermediary fees. This could be the future. This could change the world."

Karpeles began studying Bitcoin in depth. He read Satoshi Nakamoto's whitepaper, downloaded the software, and even communicated with Satoshi on the forum. He realized that Bitcoin was the opportunity he had been searching for — one that could let him fulfill his ambitions and make history.

"Bitcoin and Magic cards are similar," Karpeles later explained. "They're both digital assets, both have value, both can be traded online. But Bitcoin's market is much larger than Magic cards. If I could create a Bitcoin exchange, I could reach investors worldwide. I could become the center of this new economy."

Karpeles decided to pivot. He converted Mt. Gox from a gaming card trading platform into a Bitcoin exchange. This was more complicated than he imagined — Bitcoin's technical architecture was completely different from gaming cards, security requirements were higher, and legal risks were greater. But Karpeles was a technical genius, and he believed he could solve every problem.

"It took me three months to rewrite the entire system," Karpeles recalled. "I added Bitcoin wallet functionality, created a trade matching engine, and designed the user interface. I barely slept, working eighteen hours a day. But I knew it was worth it. This would change everything."

In July 2010, Mt. Gox officially launched. This date would be forever etched in Bitcoin's history, marking the beginning of digital currency's march from the margins to the mainstream.

### V. The Calm Before the Storm

May 21, 2010. Friday. The sky over Jacksonville was azure blue, with a gentle breeze carrying a hint of summer.

Laszlo's pizza request had been posted on the forum for four days with no response. He was starting to think maybe the idea really was too crazy. Who would be willing to accept Bitcoin? These coins had no real value, no legal protection, no physical backing. They were just numbers, just code, just the fantasy of some geeks.

He sat at his computer, fingers hovering over the delete button. Perhaps it really was a foolish idea; perhaps he should just keep mining and wait for Bitcoin to develop naturally. But just as he was about to click delete, a new reply appeared.

"I'll help you buy the pizza," wrote a user with the pseudonym "jercos." "I'll accept your Bitcoin. I'm in California and can order you a pizza from Papa John's. You'll need to provide your address."

Laszlo's heart raced. He could hardly believe his eyes. Someone was willing to accept Bitcoin! Someone was willing to trade a real product — pizza — for these digital tokens! This was a historic moment, the first step of Bitcoin moving from theory to practice.

He replied quickly: "That's great! Here's my address: [specific address in Jacksonville]. Please order two large pizzas, toppings are up to you as long as they're not too weird. I'll send the 10,000 Bitcoin to your wallet address."

They exchanged wallet addresses, and Laszlo sent the Bitcoin. The transaction was confirmed on the blockchain, 10,000 Bitcoin transferred from Laszlo's wallet to jercos's wallet. It took about an hour — the Bitcoin network was still slow back then, and block confirmations took a long time. Laszlo stared at the screen, waiting for each confirmation, filled with anticipation and nervousness.

Then jercos ordered the pizza. He spent about $25 — two large pizzas plus delivery fee and tip. For jercos, it was a good deal — he had exchanged $25 for 10,000 Bitcoin that might be worth a fortune someday. But for Laszlo, it was also a good deal — he had proven that Bitcoin could be used in the real world, and he had contributed to the development of the Bitcoin ecosystem.

That evening, Laszlo sat in his living room, looking at the two pizza boxes before him, his heart filled with complex emotions. The aroma of pizza filled the air, the cheese still steaming, the golden surface dotted with various toppings. His wife and children sat around him, curiously eyeing these "Bitcoin pizzas."

"So," his wife asked, confusion and curiosity in her eyes, "these pizzas cost you 10,000 Bitcoin?"

Laszlo nodded and took a bite of the pizza. It tasted great — rich cheese, crispy crust, abundant toppings. It was one of the best pizzas he'd ever eaten, not just because of the taste, but because of what it represented. This was the taste of history, the taste of innovation, the taste of the future.

"Yes," he said, a smile on his lips, "10,000 Bitcoin. But it was worth it. We made history. For the first time, Bitcoin was used to purchase a real product. This proves Bitcoin can work. That's worth more than money."

His wife shook her head, clearly not understanding. "I hope these coins don't become too valuable."

They all laughed. In that moment, no one believed these digital tokens would ever have real value. Ten thousand Bitcoin for two pizzas sounded like a crazy deal — a steal for jercos, an interesting experiment for Laszlo.

But history would prove this to be one of the most famous transactions in crypto history. That day, May 22, 2010, would come to be known as "Bitcoin Pizza Day." And those 10,000 Bitcoin would be worth hundreds of millions of dollars in the years to come, becoming the most expensive pizzas in human history.

Laszlo didn't know any of this. He simply enjoyed the pizza before him, sharing this special moment with his family. He didn't know that his name would be forever linked to this transaction, becoming part of crypto legend. Nor did he know that his humble dream — buying pizza with Bitcoin — would become an eternal fable about value, speculation, and human nature.

And in Tokyo, Mark Karpeles was burning the midnight oil writing code, making final preparations for his exchange. Mt. Gox was about to launch, ready to welcome its first users. A new era was about to begin — an era filled with madness, greed, dreams, and destruction.

The storm was gathering, but the sky remained calm. In this restless dawn, the turbulence of the crypto world was only beginning to show.

## Part One: The Legend of the Pizza — The Story of Laszlo Hanyecz

### I. A Programmer's Dream

Laszlo Hanyecz was not born an idealist. He grew up in an ordinary family in Florida, his father an electrician and his mother a nurse. From a young age, he showed a strong interest in electronic devices — taking apart radios, assembling computers, writing simple programs. When he was twelve, he received his first personal computer, a battered Commodore 64, and his programming career began.

"That was a magical machine," Laszlo later recalled. "Though it only had 64KB of memory, it could do so many things. I could write games on it, create music, control robots. That was the first time I realized that code could create worlds."

During high school, Laszlo excelled in math and physics, but he didn't like the rigid environment of school. He preferred to stay alone in his room, studying computer science books and finding like-minded friends online. He discovered the open source software movement and was drawn to its idealism — knowledge should be freely shared, software should have open source code, and technology should serve humanity, not corporations.

"The open source movement changed how I viewed the world," Laszlo said. "I began to believe that through collaboration and sharing, we could create something better than any corporation. Linux is the best example — a free operating system developed by volunteers, more stable, more secure, and more powerful than Microsoft's Windows."

During college, Laszlo majored in computer science, but his interests quickly exceeded the classroom. He began contributing to open source projects, submitting code on GitHub, answering questions on tech forums. He became an active member of the Linux community, helping fix bugs, improve features, and write documentation.

After graduating, Laszlo joined a tech company, but he was quickly disappointed. The company culture clashed with the open source spirit he believed in — closed source code, proprietary technology, profit-first. He felt his work was meaningless, just writing code to grow shareholders' wealth.

"I worked there for two years," Laszlo recalled. "Every morning I woke up feeling a deep emptiness. I knew I was wasting time, wasting my life. But I didn't know what other options I had. I felt like a gear in a machine — replaceable, meaningless."

The turning point came in 2008. That year, the global financial crisis erupted. Lehman Brothers collapsed, and the banking system trembled. Laszlo followed the news closely and felt deep anger and helplessness. He watched as governments used taxpayers' money to bail out banks that were "too big to fail," as ordinary people lost their homes, their jobs, and their savings, while the bankers who had caused the crisis walked away with huge bonuses, untouched.

"This is wrong," Laszlo told his wife, anger burning in his eyes. "The whole system is corrupt. Banks can print money at will, creating inflation and devaluing our savings. They can freeze our accounts, censor our transactions, control our financial lives. We entrust them with our money, but they aren't worthy of that trust. This isn't freedom — it's slavery."

His wife tried to calm him: "But this is the world we live in, Laszlo. What can one person do? One person can't change the whole system."

Laszlo didn't answer, but a seed had been planted in his heart. He began searching for an alternative — a monetary system that needed no banks, no governments, no trust. He studied the gold standard, local currencies, and mutual credit, but none satisfied him. Until the end of 2009, when he discovered Bitcoin.

### II. The Call of Bitcoin

The first time Laszlo read the Bitcoin whitepaper, it was late at night. His children were asleep, his wife was reading in the bedroom, and he sat alone in his study, staring at the computer screen. The world outside was silent, with only occasional car sounds from the distance.

When he read Satoshi Nakamoto's solution to the "double-spending problem," he felt an electric current pass through his body. His eyes widened, his heart raced, and his fingers unconsciously tightened around the mouse.

"This is brilliant," he murmured to himself, his voice echoing in the silent room. "Blockchain, proof of work, the longest chain rule — these concepts combined solve a problem none of us have been able to crack for twenty years. Satoshi is a genius. This could change everything."

He stayed up all night, downloaded the Bitcoin software, and studied the source code. He found that Satoshi was not just a theorist but also an excellent programmer. The code was clean, elegant, and secure, demonstrating deep technical skill. Every line of code was carefully designed, every function had its purpose.

More importantly, Laszlo was moved by Bitcoin's philosophy. The message Satoshi embedded in the Genesis Block — "The Times 03/Jan/2009 Chancellor on brink of second bailout for banks" — clearly indicated Bitcoin's political intent. This was a challenge to the traditional financial system, a resistance to centralized authority, a pursuit of individual freedom.

"This is what I've been searching for," Laszlo thought, hope gleaming in his eyes. "A currency that truly belongs to the people. No central bank that can print money at will, no government that can freeze accounts, no intermediaries that can charge high fees. Only mathematics and cryptography, only code and protocol. This is a tool for freedom, a weapon for justice."

From that day on, Laszlo became a true believer in Bitcoin. He became active on the forums, answering questions and sharing ideas. He improved mining software, making it more efficient and stable. He wrote documentation to help newcomers understand how Bitcoin worked. He became a core member of the community, though few knew his true identity.

"I wasn't in it for the money," Laszlo later explained. "At least not at first. I was in it for the ideals. I believed Bitcoin could change the world — provide financial services to the unbanked, protect privacy, and combat inflation. This was a righteous cause, the mission of our generation."

But over time, Laszlo also began thinking about Bitcoin's practical applications. Theory was one thing; practice was another. If Bitcoin could only be passed between geeks and could never be used to purchase real-world goods, it would remain a toy, not a true currency.

"We need a breakthrough," Laszlo wrote on the forum. "We need to prove that Bitcoin can be used in the real world. We need someone willing to accept Bitcoin as a means of payment. Without real usage, Bitcoin is just a concept, an experiment, a toy."

That's why he made that famous pizza request. Not for fun, not for fame, but to prove a point: Bitcoin could work, could become a medium of exchange, could connect the digital world with the real world.

"I know 10,000 Bitcoin sounds like a lot," Laszlo later explained. "But at the time, these coins really had no value. There were no exchanges, no market prices, no one could tell you what one Bitcoin was worth in dollars. They were just numbers, just code. Using them to buy pizza, for me, was like an interesting experiment, a proof of concept. I wanted to prove that Bitcoin was useful, that it could change the world."

### III. Behind the Pizza Transaction

Laszlo's pizza request sat on the Bitcointalk forum for days without attracting much attention at first. Most people thought it was a joke or some kind of crazy stunt. Who would trade 10,000 Bitcoin for two pizzas? These coins were worthless, maybe always would be. The idea was too ahead of its time, too idealistic, too impractical.

But Laszlo was serious. He kept bumping his post, asking if anyone was interested. In replies, he explained that he wasn't crazy — he wanted to demonstrate Bitcoin's practicality. He believed that if someone was willing to accept Bitcoin for pizza, it would be an important milestone in Bitcoin's development.

"If I can buy pizza with Bitcoin," he wrote, "that proves Bitcoin can function as currency. This is an important step in growing the ecosystem. We need to prove that Bitcoin is not just a technical experiment — it can become a real medium of exchange."

Finally, on May 21, 2010, jercos appeared.

Jercos's real name was Jeremy Sturdivant, only nineteen years old at the time, living in California. He was an early Bitcoin adopter who was also active on the forum. When he saw Laszlo's request, he thought it was an interesting opportunity, a chance to participate in history.

"At first I also thought it was a joke," jercos later recalled. "But Laszlo looked serious. I thought, why not? I had $25, I could buy him a pizza. If those Bitcoin become worth something someday, it would be a windfall. If not, I just spent money on takeout. There was no real risk for me."

Jercos and Laszlo exchanged contact information through forum private messages. They discussed the details — pizza toppings, delivery address, how to send the Bitcoin. The whole process was simple, but also a bit nerve-wracking. After all, this was the first real-world transaction conducted with Bitcoin; there was no precedent to follow, no intermediary to trust. Every step was filled with uncertainty.

"I remember when I sent the Bitcoin," Laszlo said. "I stared at the screen, waiting for confirmation. The network was slow back then, and transactions took a long time to confirm. I was worried something would go wrong, worried jercos wouldn't actually order the pizza, worried everything would fall apart. But every step went smoothly, like magic."

After the transaction was confirmed on the blockchain, jercos sprang into action. He logged onto Papa John's website, selected two large pizzas — Italian sausage, mushrooms, onions, and green peppers, standard toppings. He entered Laszlo's address, paid $25, and waited for the pizza to arrive.

"When the doorbell rang and I saw the delivery driver holding the pizza boxes," Laszlo recalled, excitement flickering in his eyes, "I felt an indescribable thrill. This wasn't just pizza — this was history. For the first time ever, Bitcoin was used to purchase a real product. We proved it was possible. Bitcoin actually works."

That night, Laszlo took a photo — two pizza boxes sitting next to his computer keyboard, the Bitcoin wallet interface displayed on the screen. He posted this photo on the forum as proof of the transaction. This photo would later become one of the most famous images in crypto history.

"I just wanted to prove I'd done it," Laszlo wrote. "I successfully bought two pizzas with 10,000 Bitcoin. Thanks, jercos! This is an important step for the Bitcoin ecosystem."

The post caused a sensation on the forum. Replies flooded in, congratulating Laszlo and praising the historical significance of the transaction. Some joked that it was "the most expensive pizza in human history," though no one back then realized how prophetic those words would be. Others began discussing Bitcoin's future, more use cases, and how to make Bitcoin a real currency.

### IV. An Ordinary Daily Life

After the pizza transaction, Laszlo's life didn't change much. He continued his daily routine — coding at the company during the day, mining and studying Bitcoin at night. His Bitcoin reserves continued to grow, because as one of the earliest adopters of graphics processing unit (GPU) mining, he had a significant hashing power advantage over other miners.

"Mining was easy back then," Laszlo recalled. "I could mine thousands of coins a day with my graphics cards. The block reward was 50 BTC, with a new block every ten minutes. If I was lucky, I could mine several blocks in a day. Competition wasn't that fierce yet — regular computers could still mine coins."

Laszlo's enthusiasm for Bitcoin affected his family. Though his wife didn't fully understand the technical details, she supported her husband's hobby. She would buy him coffee when he stayed up late mining; she would take care of the kids so he had time to study code; she would tolerate the noise and heat in his study, understanding it was where his passion lay.

"She thought I was a bit crazy," Laszlo said with a laugh. "But she saw my passion. She knew I truly believed in this thing, not just for the money. She saw me doing what I believed was right, and that made her proud."

Laszlo's children also became interested in Bitcoin. They would play in their father's study, listening to the hum of the computer fans, watching the numbers dance on the screen. To them, it was just dad's strange hobby, like how some people liked collecting stamps or fishing. But they could also sense their father's excitement, that feeling of being part of history.

"My oldest son was five then," Laszlo said. "He would ask, 'Daddy, what are you doing?' I would say, 'I'm mining digital gold coins.' He would say, 'Can we buy toys with them?' I would say, 'Maybe someday.' Looking back now, those 'digital gold coins' could have bought a toy factory."

Laszlo continued making small transactions with his Bitcoin. He would send Bitcoin to other forum users as thanks or tips. He would try buying other goods with Bitcoin — once, he successfully bought a pair of socks with Bitcoin from another early adopter. Every transaction was proof of Bitcoin's practicality, every one pushing the ecosystem forward.

"I wanted to prove Bitcoin could function as currency," Laszlo explained. "Not just as a one-time pizza transaction, but as something used continuously, like cash or a credit card. I wanted people to see that Bitcoin was real, useful, and capable of changing the world."

But over time, Laszlo faced a dilemma. Bitcoin's price began to rise. By the end of 2010, the first Bitcoin exchange appeared, and the price rose from zero to a few cents. In 2011, the price broke through $1, then continued climbing. The value of Laszlo's Bitcoin holdings rose from hundreds of dollars to thousands, then to tens of thousands.

"That was strange," Laszlo recalled. "I suddenly realized that these digital tokens I held had real value. I could sell them, buy a car, buy a house, pay off the mortgage. But I didn't. I felt that doing so would be a betrayal of my beliefs. I believed Bitcoin was the currency of the future, not a speculative tool. I wanted to hold onto them until Bitcoin truly changed the world."

Laszlo continued holding his Bitcoin, continued mining, continued contributing to the community. But as the price rose, his mindset also began to shift. The pure idealism of the early days started to be influenced by practical considerations. He began worrying about security — if his computer was hacked, if his wallet was stolen, if his hard drive was corrupted, he could lose everything.

"I remember once, my hard drive had a problem," Laszlo said, his expression turning serious. "I thought I had lost all my Bitcoin. That was one of the most panicked moments of my life. I spent hours trying to recover the data, my hands shaking, my heart about to burst out of my chest. Fortunately, I recovered the data, but that experience made me realize how fragile these digital assets are."

That experience prompted Laszlo to manage his Bitcoin more carefully. He moved most of his coins to cold storage — an offline storage method where private keys are kept on a computer not connected to the internet. He made multiple backups, stored in different locations. He sold some coins, exchanging them for cash as "insurance" — in case Bitcoin went to zero.

"I'm not a gambler," Laszlo explained. "I believe in Bitcoin, but I also need to think about my family. If Bitcoin fails, I can't let my kids go hungry. So I sold some coins, enough to pay some bills, but kept most of them. I wanted to find a balance between ideals and reality."

### V. History's Judgment

In 2011, Bitcoin's price broke through $1. Those 10,000 pizza Bitcoin were worth $10,000 — 400 times the actual cost of the pizza. The media and internet forums began paying attention to the story, and Laszlo became known as "that idiot who paid $10,000 for a pizza."

"People started mocking me," Laszlo recalled. "They said I was the worst negotiator in history, that I should have held onto those coins, that I was an idiot. They didn't understand the context, didn't understand that Bitcoin had no market price at the time. They only saw the result, not the process."

Laszlo tried to explain that he hadn't done anything wrong at the moment of the transaction. At the time of the trade, Bitcoin had no market price, no exchange, no liquidity. Those coins were just numbers, just code, just part of the experiment. Using them to buy pizza was to demonstrate Bitcoin's practicality, to push the ecosystem's development.

"If early adopters hadn't been willing to spend their Bitcoin," Laszlo wrote, "Bitcoin would never have become a currency. If everyone just hoarded and waited for the price to rise, Bitcoin would have been nothing more than a speculative tool, not a medium of exchange. I'm proud to have been the first person to use Bitcoin to buy a real product. I helped make history."

But the public didn't care about these explanations. They only saw the result — Laszlo spent 10,000 Bitcoin on two pizzas, and those Bitcoin later became worth hundreds of millions of dollars. It was a perfect story, a modern fable about greed, foolishness, and missed opportunities. It fit people's expectations of "fools and their money" and "the clever undone by their own cleverness."

As time went on, Bitcoin's price continued to rise. In 2013, it broke through $100, and the pizza Bitcoin were worth a million dollars. In 2017, it broke through $10,000, and the pizza Bitcoin were worth a hundred million dollars. In 2021, it broke through $60,000, and the pizza Bitcoin were worth six hundred million dollars.

Every time the price rose, Laszlo's story was revisited. Media outlets would interview him, asking if he regretted it. Netizens would mock him, holding him up as a典型案例 of failed speculation. Bitcoin's critics would use his story to prove Bitcoin was a bubble, a speculative frenzy, a worthless digital game.

### VI. The Irony of Fate

Laszlo's story became a cautionary tale for speculators. It reminds people that markets are unpredictable, greed is dangerous, and FOMO (Fear Of Missing Out) leads to poor decisions. ## Part Two: The Rise of Exchanges — The Birth of Mt. Gox

### I. Mark Karpeles's Ambition

While Laszlo was enjoying his pizza, on the other side of the Pacific, a young man was making final preparations for his dream. This dream would change Bitcoin's history, bring unprecedented liquidity to digital currency, and sow the seeds of future catastrophe.

Mark Karpeles sat in his apartment in Tokyo's Shibuya district, surrounded by energy drink cans and takeout boxes. His eyes were bloodshot, but ambition gleamed in them. He had been working continuously for seventy-two hours, just to complete Mt. Gox's final调试. The air in the room was stale and stifling, but his attention was completely focused on the screen.

"This will change the world," Karpeles said to himself, his voice hoarse but full of passion. "People will buy and sell Bitcoin here, as easily as trading stocks. I will create the world's largest Bitcoin exchange. I will stand at the center of the new economy."

Karpeles was twenty-four years old, but he had already experienced many ups and downs in life. He was born in Dijon, France, a city famous for its mustard. From a young age, he showed a talent for computers. At ten, he was already capable of writing complex programs; by sixteen, he was already a well-known figure in the French hacker community.

But there was something peculiar about his personality. Karpeles was introverted and socially awkward. He was frequently bullied at school because he was overweight, clumsy, and preferred computers to sports. These experiences shaped his character — he became sensitive, self-important, and desperate to prove himself.

"I was very lonely as a child," Karpeles later admitted. "My computer was my only friend. In the world of code, I was king. I could create anything, control anything. That was the only time I felt powerful."

At eighteen, Karpeles left school and began working as a full-time programmer. He joined several tech companies but didn't stay long at any of them. He disliked being told what to do, disliked corporate bureaucracy, disliked the nine-to-five life. He dreamed of starting his own business and creating an empire of his own.

In 2006, Karpeles came to Japan. He was drawn to the country's culture — anime, gaming, technology, order. Japan was a paradise for tech enthusiasts, the frontier of digital innovation. He found work at a tech company in Tokyo, but quickly grew bored. In 2008, he founded his first company — Tibanne K.K., providing web hosting and server management services.

Tibanne didn't achieve tremendous success, but it taught Karpeles how to run a company. He learned how to handle servers, manage clients, and deal with technical issues. These experiences would prove useful in the future.

### II. From Gaming Cards to Digital Currency

At the end of 2009, Karpeles first heard about Bitcoin. At the time, he was developing a platform called "Magic: The Gathering Online eXchange," or Mt. Gox — an online marketplace for trading virtual gaming cards.

Magic: The Gathering was a popular trading card game with millions of players. These virtual cards had value, and players were willing to buy and sell them with real money. Karpeles's idea was to create an exchange where players could trade cards like stocks.

"The idea was great," Karpeles thought. "But it had a problem — the scale was too small. Though the Magic player base was large, the number of people willing to trade cards online was limited. I needed to find a bigger market."

It was then that he discovered Bitcoin.

Karpeles spent several weeks studying Bitcoin. He read the whitepaper, downloaded the software, and even communicated with Satoshi Nakamoto on the forum. He realized that Bitcoin was the opportunity he had been searching for.

"Bitcoin and Magic cards are similar," Karpeles later explained. "They're both digital assets, both have value, both can be traded online. But Bitcoin's market is much larger than Magic cards. If I could create a Bitcoin exchange, I could reach investors worldwide. I could become the center of this new economy."

Karpeles decided to pivot. He converted Mt. Gox from a gaming card trading platform into a Bitcoin exchange. This was more complicated than he imagined — Bitcoin's technical architecture was completely different from gaming cards, security requirements were higher, and legal risks were greater. But Karpeles was a technical genius, and he believed he could solve every problem.

"It took me three months to rewrite the entire system," Karpeles recalled. "I added Bitcoin wallet functionality, created a trade matching engine, and designed the user interface. I barely slept, working eighteen hours a day. But I knew it was worth it. This would change everything."

In July 2010, Mt. Gox officially launched.

### III. The Dawn of Exchanges

Mt. Gox's launch was an important moment in Bitcoin history. It was the first fully functional Bitcoin exchange, where people could buy and sell Bitcoin with real money.

The exchange's operation was simple: users registered an account, deposited dollars, yen, or euros into Mt. Gox's bank account, and then used these funds to purchase Bitcoin. Conversely, users could also sell Bitcoin and withdraw the proceeds to their own bank account. Mt. Gox charged a 0.6% fee on each transaction.

"Before Mt. Gox appeared, buying and selling Bitcoin was very difficult," an early user recalled. "You had to find someone willing to trade on a forum, then pay via PayPal or bank transfer. This required trust and carried high risk. Mt. Gox made everything simple — you just clicked a few buttons and could buy or sell Bitcoin."

Mt. Gox's emergence greatly promoted Bitcoin's adoption. Now, anyone could easily obtain Bitcoin — no mining required, no technical knowledge needed, no need to trust strangers. This brought a large influx of new users to Bitcoin and drove prices upward.

When Mt. Gox launched in July 2010, Bitcoin's price was about $0.05. By the end of 2010, the price had risen to $0.30. In February 2011, the price broke through $1. By June 2011, it reached an all-time high of $31.

"Mt. Gox was a catalyst for the price rise," an early investor said. "It made Bitcoin accessible and allowed investors to easily enter and exit the market. This brought liquidity, brought demand, brought price increases."

But for Karpeles, running an exchange was not easy. He was essentially one person running the entire platform — handling technical issues, customer support, banking relationships, and security threats. He worked eighteen hours a day, dealing with countless problems.

"The hardest part was banking," Karpeles recalled. "Most banks didn't want to do business with Bitcoin companies. They thought Bitcoin was a money laundering tool, a medium for illegal transactions. I had to search everywhere for banks willing to accept us, pay high fees, and comply with strict regulations."

Security was also a huge challenge. Bitcoin exchanges were prime targets for hackers. If hackers could breach the system, they could steal users' Bitcoin, and these transactions were irreversible. Karpeles knew that one major hack could destroy his exchange.

"I spent a lot of time strengthening security," Karpeles said. "I encrypted all data, implemented multi-signature authorization, and set up cold wallets to store most of the funds. But I was always worried, worried that someday hackers would find a vulnerability, worried I would lose everything."

### IV. The Shadow of Prosperity

Despite the challenges, Mt. Gox experienced explosive growth between 2010 and 2013. It became the world's largest Bitcoin exchange, processing over 70% of all Bitcoin transactions worldwide. Every day, millions of dollars flowed through the platform, and hundreds of thousands of users bought and sold Bitcoin there.

Karpeles transformed from a lonely programmer to the emperor of the cryptocurrency world. He moved into luxurious offices, hired dozens of employees, and gave interviews to the media. He appeared on magazine covers, described as "the banker of Bitcoin" and "the pioneer of digital currency."

"It was a crazy time," Karpeles recalled. "Every morning I woke up to find that the number of users had increased by thousands again, that trading volume had hit another new high. Money flooded in like a tide — fee revenue, advertising revenue, investment funds. I felt invincible."

But this prosperity concealed serious problems.

First, Karpeles was not a good manager. He was a technical genius but lacked business experience. He didn't know how to build an effective organizational structure, how to manage finances, or how to deal with regulation. Mt. Gox's operations were chaotic — accounts were unclear, internal controls were lacking, and employee turnover was high.

"Mark was a good guy, very smart," a former employee said. "But he wasn't good at management. He would suddenly change his mind, make impulsive decisions. He didn't trust anyone and tried to do everything himself. This led to a lot of confusion and mistakes."

Second, Mt. Gox's technical architecture had serious flaws. The system was written in PHP, a language not suited for handling high-concurrency trading. The database design was poor, frequently crashing. Security vulnerabilities were common — though most were discovered and fixed in time, the risk always lingered.

"The system was like a house of cards," a security expert later commented. "It looked impressive from the outside, but inside it was full of cracks. Any competent hacker could find a way in."

Third, Karpeles himself had problems. His lifestyle became extravagant — buying expensive electronics, dining at high-end Tokyo restaurants, living in luxurious apartments. He began to distance himself from early supporters and friends, becoming arrogant and isolated.

"Success changed Mark," an old friend recalled. "He used to be humble, always willing to help others. But after success, he changed. He thought he was a genius, invincible. He stopped listening to others' opinions and only trusted himself."

The accumulation of these problems would lead to catastrophic consequences in the years to come. But during the boom period of 2010-2011, no one saw the coming storm. Everyone was celebrating Bitcoin's success, celebrating Mt. Gox's growth, celebrating the arrival of the digital gold age.

### V. The Price of Power

As the head of the world's largest Bitcoin exchange, Karpeles wielded enormous power. He could decide which transactions were processed, which users were frozen, which rules were implemented. He controlled the funds of hundreds of thousands of users — their financial fates depended on his decisions.

This power was seductive, but also dangerous.

"I remember once," an early user recalled, "my account was frozen without any explanation. I contacted customer service, but nobody replied. I contacted Mark, and he just said 'system error,' then unfroze my account. But it took him three days. During those three days, I watched Bitcoin's price rise from $15 to $20 but couldn't trade. I lost thousands of dollars."

Similar complaints proliferated on the forums. Users complained about Mt. Gox's terrible customer service, withdrawal delays, and accounts frozen without cause. Some suspected Karpeles was manipulating the market, using insider information for personal gain.

"We have no evidence," one user wrote. "But something is strange. Why before every major price surge does Mt. Gox's 'cold wallet' have large Bitcoin transfers? Why does Karpeles always buy and sell at the perfect times?"

Karpeles denied these accusations. He said Mt. Gox was completely transparent and he had not participated in any market manipulation. But the seeds of suspicion had been planted, and the community's trust in him began to erode.

Meanwhile, regulators also began to take notice of Mt. Gox. The U.S. Department of Homeland Security, Japan's Financial Services Agency, the European Banking Authority — all were studying how to regulate Bitcoin exchanges. They worried about money laundering, tax evasion, and terrorist financing. Mt. Gox became a target for regulation.

"We received countless requests," Karpeles recalled. "Requests for user information, requests to report suspicious transactions, requests to comply with anti-money laundering regulations. I wasn't sure we could meet all these requirements. It made me very anxious."

Karpeles tried to cooperate with regulators. He hired lawyers, implemented KYC (Know Your Customer) policies, and reported suspicious transactions. But it wasn't enough. Regulatory pressure kept building, and Mt. Gox's legal risks kept rising.

"Sometimes I thought," Karpeles said in an interview, "if I had known running an exchange would be this difficult, I might never have started. But I had no way back. Too many people depended on Mt. Gox, too much money was on the platform. I had to continue, whatever the cost."

### VI. Cracks in the Empire

In June 2011, Mt. Gox experienced its first major crisis.

A hacker breached Karpeles's personal computer and stole his administrator password. Then, the hacker logged into Mt. Gox's system, created an account, and began selling a large amount of Bitcoin. This caused Bitcoin's price to plummet from $17 to $0.01 in an instant, then rebound quickly.

"It was a terrifying night," Karpeles recalled. "I was asleep when I got a phone call saying the system had crashed. I logged in to check and found the price had crashed, millions of dollars' worth of Bitcoin had been sold at absurd prices. I didn't know what was happening, didn't know how big the losses were."

Fortunately, Mt. Gox had a "rollback" mechanism. Karpeles was able to reverse the hacker's transactions and restore the system to its pre-attack state. But losses were still significant — the hacker successfully withdrew some Bitcoin and cash, totaling about a few thousand dollars' worth.

"This attack was a wake-up call," Karpeles said afterward. "It exposed how vulnerable our system was. We had to strengthen security, had to be more careful."

But Mt. Gox didn't truly learn its lesson. Security vulnerabilities continued to appear; though most were discovered and fixed in time, the risk always remained. Worse still, this attack revealed the chaos of Mt. Gox's internal management — administrator passwords were stored on unsecured computers, there was insufficient multi-factor authentication, and audit logs were incomplete.

"If someone had seriously investigated back then," a security expert later commented, "they would have found how terrible Mt. Gox's management was. But nobody investigated. The Bitcoin community was too excited, too focused on rising prices, too trusting of Mark Karpeles. They ignored the warning signs."

The warning signs continued to appear. In 2012, Mt. Gox's bank accounts were frozen by the U.S. government on suspicion of money laundering. In 2013, the U.S. Department of Homeland Security seized Mt. Gox's bank accounts, preventing users from withdrawing funds for weeks. With each crisis, Karpeles managed to survive, but his reputation was gradually damaged and user trust was gradually eroding.

"I started to worry," a long-time user said. "Mt. Gox looked too big, too important to fail. But if it did fail, what would happen? What would happen to the Bitcoin I had deposited there?"

These questions would be answered in 2014, but that is a story for later. During the boom period of 2010-2013, Mt. Gox continued to dominate the Bitcoin market, and Karpeles continued to enjoy his power and wealth. The cracks in the empire were already showing, but nobody wanted to face them. The feast continued, until the final collapse.

## Part Three: Prosperity and Madness — The Price Surge of 2010-2011

### I. From Priceless to Valuable

On May 22, 2010, when Laszlo Hanyecz used 10,000 Bitcoin to buy two pizzas, this was not merely an amusing anecdote. It was a turning point in Bitcoin's history, marking this digital currency's transition from zero value to having value.

Before the pizza transaction, Bitcoin had no market price. It was an experimental digital currency circulated only among geeks, with no connection to the real-world economy. You could mine it, send it, receive it, but you couldn't buy anything with it, couldn't exchange it for dollars or euros.

The pizza transaction changed this. It established a benchmark — two pizzas were worth about $25, so 10,000 Bitcoin were worth about $25, making one Bitcoin worth about $0.0025. This was the first time Bitcoin was assigned a dollar value in its history.

"The pizza transaction created a price anchor," an economist later analyzed. "Once people knew how much one Bitcoin was worth, they could begin trading, begin speculating, begin assigning it more value. This is a classic phenomenon in monetary theory — value is subjective, but once a price is established, it reinforces itself."

After the pizza transaction, the Bitcoin community began discussing price. People posted on forums asking "how much is Bitcoin worth today" and "how many dollars should I spend on Bitcoin." Some early adopters began making small transactions with Bitcoin — buying socks, coffee, books. Each transaction reinforced Bitcoin's value.

When Mt. Gox launched in July 2010, Bitcoin's price was about $0.05 — a 20-fold increase from the pizza transaction. By the end of 2010, the price rose to $0.30. In February 2011, it broke through $1. By June 2011, it reached an all-time high of $31.

This price increase was exponential and dizzying. Early adopters watched their "digital wallets" grow from a few hundred dollars to thousands, then to tens of thousands. Most of them had never seen wealth grow so quickly.

"I remember the first time I saw my Bitcoin holdings exceed $10,000," an early miner recalled. "I could barely believe it. Months ago, these coins were worthless. Now, I could buy a car with them. It was insane."

### II. The Influx of Speculators

The price rise attracted speculators. They didn't come for Bitcoin's ideals, didn't come for the dream of decentralization — they came to make money. They saw Bitcoin's price rising quickly and wanted a piece of the action.

Between late 2010 and early 2011, a noticeable change occurred in the Bitcoin community. New voices appeared on the forums — not discussing technology, but discussing price; not sharing ideas, but sharing trading tips. People began using terms like "HODL" (Hold On for Dear Life), began discussing "to the moon," and began worshipping those who had gotten rich through Bitcoin.

"I remember a post," an old user recalled, "where someone asked, 'What are Bitcoin's fundamentals?' Someone replied, 'The fundamentals are that the price goes up.' That was the mentality back then — no need to understand the technology, no need to believe in the ideals, just believe the price would rise."

The influx of speculators drove prices higher but also brought volatility. Bitcoin's price began to fluctuate wildly, rising or falling 20%, 30%, or even 50% in a single day. This volatility attracted even more speculators — those who loved high risk and high reward, those who believed they could "buy low and sell high."

"I bought my first Bitcoin in March 2011," a speculator later recalled. "The price was $0.90 at the time. I bought $1,000 worth of Bitcoin, about 1,100 coins. A month later, the price rose to $3, and my investment became $3,300. Two months later, the price rose to $15, and my investment became $16,500. I couldn't believe it. I'd never seen returns like that."

But these returns came at a cost. Bitcoin's price was extremely volatile, and many people were wiped out. Those who bought at $15 might panic-sell when the price dropped to $10, losing 33%. Those who bought at $10 might sell at $20, then watch the price continue rising to $30, filled with regret.

"It was a psychological warfare," a trader said. "Everyone was guessing what everyone else was thinking, trying to predict the next move in price. FOMO drove you to buy; FUD (Fear, Uncertainty, Doubt) drove you to sell. It was a zero-sum game — for every winner there was a loser."

### III. The Birth of Millionaires

The Bitcoin bull market of 2011 created a cohort of early millionaires. Those who had mined or bought Bitcoin in the earliest days watched their wealth grow to unbelievable levels.

"I remember in June 2011," an early adopter recalled, "my Bitcoin holdings exceeded $1 million. Two years earlier, I was just an ordinary programmer with an ordinary salary. Now, I was a millionaire. It changed my life."

These early millionaire stories became part of Bitcoin legend. They were covered by the media, revered by the community, and seen as prophets and heroes. Their stories attracted even more people into Bitcoin, driving prices even higher.

But wealth also brought problems. Many early millionaires didn't know how to manage their newfound wealth. They began living lavishly — buying luxury cars, mansions, yachts. They became targets for scammers and hackers. Their personal relationships became strained — friends and family began asking to borrow money or grew jealous of their success.

"Wealth changed everything," an early millionaire later admitted. "My old friends started looking at me differently. Some wanted to borrow money, some wanted to invest, some were just jealous. I had to learn to say no, to protect my wealth, to distinguish real friends from fair-weather ones."

Tax issues were even more complicated. In 2011, most countries' tax authorities didn't have clear Bitcoin tax policies. Early millionaires didn't know if they needed to pay taxes on Bitcoin gains, or if they did, how to report them. Some chose to ignore the tax issue, hoping the government wouldn't notice. Others hired lawyers and accountants, trying to comply with the law, only to find it was vague and unclear.

"I sold some Bitcoin in 2011," an early adopter said. "I didn't know I needed to pay taxes on the gains. Three years later, the tax authority contacted me, demanding back taxes plus penalties and interest. That almost wiped out all my profits. It was a painful lesson."

### IV. Warnings of a Bubble

As prices rose rapidly, some voices began warning of a bubble. Economists, financial experts, and even some of Bitcoin's early supporters began questioning the sustainability of this price increase.

"This is tulip mania all over again," an economist warned. "Bitcoin has no intrinsic value, no cash flow, no asset backing. Its price is entirely based on speculation, on people's belief that prices will continue rising. Once that belief wavers, the bubble will burst."

Bitcoin's supporters countered that Bitcoin had a unique value proposition — decentralization, limited supply, global portability. They argued that the price increase reflected the market's recognition of Bitcoin's value, not a speculative bubble.

"Bitcoin is not a tulip," a supporter wrote. "It's a new technology, a new currency, a new asset class. Its value comes from its practicality, its scarcity, its network effects. Prices may fluctuate, but in the long run, Bitcoin's value will only grow."

This debate continues to this day. But in 2011, Bitcoin's price seemed to prove the supporters right. Despite the warnings, prices kept rising, more people flooded in, and more merchants began accepting Bitcoin.

"It was hard to stay rational back then," an investor later recalled. "Everyone was making money, everyone believed Bitcoin would change the world. If you questioned it, you were a heretic, you didn't understand technology, you were just jealous of others' success. This groupthink was very powerful and very dangerous."

### V. Harbingers of the Crash

In June 2011, Bitcoin's price reached an all-time high of $31, then began a violent correction. By November 2011, the price had fallen to $2 — a decline of over 90%.

This crash had many causes. The Mt. Gox hack sparked panic. U.S. government regulatory actions increased uncertainty. More importantly, the early speculative bubble had become overinflated and needed to correct.

"I remember those months," an investor recalled. "Every morning I woke up to see the price had dropped again. I tried to hold on, believing the price would rebound, but it just kept falling. I watched my wealth evaporate and felt despair and rage."

Many people lost heavily in the crash. Those who bought at $30 watched their investments shrink by 90%. Those who had borrowed money to invest fell into debt crises. Those who had been confident at the peak were broken at the bottom.

"I sold all my Bitcoin," an investor wrote after the crash. "I lost 80%, but I couldn't take any more. I needed to preserve what I had left and start over. Bitcoin was a scam, a bubble, and I would never touch it again."

But the crash also created opportunities. Those who bought at $2 reaped huge returns in the years that followed. Bitcoin's price stabilized in 2012, then surged again in 2013, reaching over $1,000.

"The crash was painful, but it was also necessary," a long-term holder said. "It cleared out the speculators and left the true believers. It brought prices back to reality and established a stronger foundation. Without the 2011 crash, there would have been no 2013 boom."

### VI. The Philosophy of Value

Bitcoin's price volatility during 2010-2011 raised a profound philosophical question: What is value?

In economics, value is subjective. Something has value because people believe it has value. Gold has value not because of its industrial uses, but because people believe it is precious. The dollar has value not because the paper itself is worth anything, but because people believe the government will support it.

Bitcoin's value works the same way. It has no physical form, no government backing, no cash flow. Its value comes entirely from people's beliefs — belief that it will become the currency of the future, belief that others will accept it, belief that the price will continue rising.

"Bitcoin is the ultimate experiment in value," a philosopher commented. "It tests our understanding of money, value, and trust. If Bitcoin succeeds, it will prove that value can exist entirely based on belief, without any physical backing. This will change our understanding of economics, society, and politics."

But this also brings risks. If belief wavers, value collapses. Bitcoin's wild price swings are precisely because its value is entirely belief-based, with no "fundamentals" to anchor it.

"That's the nature of speculation," an economist said. "You buy something not because it has intrinsic value, but because you believe someone else will buy it from you at a higher price. This is the Greater Fool Theory — as long as there's a bigger fool than you, you can make money. But someday, the last fool will be left holding the bag, and the bubble will burst."

Laszlo Hanyecz's pizza transaction, in this context, takes on new meaning. He spent 10,000 Bitcoin on two pizzas, not because Bitcoin had no value, but because at that moment, Bitcoin's value was exactly two pizzas. This was the starting point of value, the beginning of belief.

"The absurdity of value is that it's circular," a thinker wrote. "Bitcoin has value because people are willing to trade with it. People are willing to trade with it because it has value. This is a self-fulfilling prophecy, a cycle of belief. If that cycle breaks, value disappears."

The 2011 price volatility was both a demonstration of this cycle and a warning. Bitcoin rose from $0.0025 to $31, proving the power of belief. Then it fell from $31 to $2, proving belief's fragility.

"This is the nature of the crypto world," an old user summarized. "The wind rises from the edges of duckweed, the clouds gather at the horizon. Value is born in belief, collapses in doubt, and is reborn in despair. An eternal cycle, a dance that never stops."

## Part Four: Omens — The First Hacking Crisis

### I. The First Dark Arrow

June 19, 2011. Monday. 3:00 AM, Tokyo time.

Mark Karpeles was awakened by his phone ringing. He groggily picked it up and heard his technical support staff's trembling voice: "Mark, something's happened. The system has been breached. The price is crashing."

Karpeles snapped awake instantly. He jumped out of bed, ran to his computer, and logged into Mt. Gox's administrative interface. What he saw made his blood freeze — Bitcoin's price had plummeted from $17 to $0.01 in an instant, then rebounded within seconds. The trading records showed someone had sold a massive amount of Bitcoin at virtually zero price.

"What happened?" Karpeles shouted, panic flooding his voice.

The investigation revealed that a hacker had breached Karpeles's personal computer and stolen his Mt. Gox administrator password. Then the hacker logged into the system, created a new account, and began selling Bitcoin. Due to Mt. Gox's trade matching mechanism, these low-priced sell orders were executed, causing the price to plummet instantly.

"I made a fatal mistake," Karpeles later admitted. "I stored my administrator password on my personal computer without enabling multi-factor authentication. I was too confident, thought no one could breach my system. I was wrong."

The attack lasted about an hour. During this time, the hacker sold approximately 2,609 Bitcoin and received about $3,000 in cash, then attempted to withdraw these funds. But Mt. Gox's withdrawal system had delays, and the hacker only successfully withdrew part of the funds.

Karpeles took emergency measures. He froze all withdrawals, shut down the trading system, and began investigating the losses. He found that though the price had crashed, most transactions could be "rolled back" — the hacker's transactions during the attack could be reversed, restoring the system to its pre-attack state.

"The rollback was controversial," Karpeles said. "In traditional financial markets, transactions are irreversible. But Bitcoin is different. If we didn't roll back, many users would lose large amounts of money. We had to protect them."

Mt. Gox rolled back the transactions during the attack, stripping the profits of those lucky buyers who had purchased Bitcoin at $0.01. This caused some controversy — those buyers complained they hadn't done anything wrong, so why should they be denied their profits? But most people understood it was a necessary measure to prevent system collapse.

### II. The Vulnerability of the System

The attack exposed serious vulnerabilities in Mt. Gox's system.

First, the security architecture had fundamental flaws. Administrator passwords should never be stored on any internet-connected computer. There should have been strict access controls, multi-factor authentication, and comprehensive audit logs. Mt. Gox had none of these.

Second, the trade matching mechanism had vulnerabilities. The system should not have allowed prices to plummet 99% in an instant — there should have been circuit breakers to prevent extreme volatility. Mt. Gox had no such protections.

Third, risk management was absent. Mt. Gox had no emergency response plan, no crisis management process, no backup systems. When the attack occurred, Karpeles could only respond by instinct, with no time to consider the best course of action.

"We were a system held together with duct tape and string," a former employee later admitted. "It worked under normal conditions, but against an attack it was like a house of cards. I'm surprised we weren't breached sooner."

The attack also exposed Karpeles's personal management problems. He tried to do everything himself, didn't trust others, and hadn't built an effective team. This created a single point of failure — if Karpeles's computer was breached, the entire system was exposed.

"Mark was a control freak," a friend said. "He wanted to control everything and didn't want to delegate to anyone. This might have worked in the early days, but when the company grew, it became a liability. One person can't manage a global exchange — it's not possible."

### III. Cracks in Trust

Although Mt. Gox successfully rolled back the attack, users' trust had already begun to erode.

"If hackers could so easily breach the system," one user wrote on the forum, "are our funds even safe? What if the next attack is even worse? What if Mark can't stop it?"

There were no answers to these questions. Karpeles promised to strengthen security, hire more technical staff, and improve the system architecture. But users no longer fully trusted him. Some began transferring their Bitcoin from Mt. Gox to personal wallets, reducing their exposure to exchange risk.

"I started to worry," a long-time user said. "Mt. Gox looked too big, too important to fail. But what if it did fail? What would happen to the Bitcoin I had deposited there?"

These concerns were justified. Mt. Gox held users' Bitcoin, but these Bitcoin were actually controlled by Mt. Gox. Users only had digital records of account balances, not the private keys to their Bitcoin. If Mt. Gox went bankrupt or was hacked, users might never recover their funds.

"This is the fundamental problem with centralized exchanges," a cryptographer warned. "You entrust your assets to a third party, trusting they will take good care of them. But history tells us this trust is often misplaced. Banks fail, exchanges get hacked, funds disappear. Bitcoin was invented to avoid this, but we've returned to square one."

### IV. The Significance of Omens

The June 2011 hack was an omen of Mt. Gox's fate. It presaged an even larger collapse to come, though at the time no one realized it.

"If we had seriously investigated back then," a security expert later commented, "we would have immediately shut down Mt. Gox and demanded Karpeles undergo a comprehensive audit and establish effective oversight. But we didn't. The Bitcoin community was too excited, too focused on rising prices, too trusting of Mark Karpeles. We ignored the warning signs."

The warning signs continued to appear. In 2012, Mt. Gox's bank accounts were frozen by the U.S. government. In 2013, withdrawal delays became the norm, with users complaining they had to wait weeks to withdraw funds. With each crisis, Karpeles managed to survive, but Mt. Gox's reputation was gradually damaged and user trust was gradually eroding.

"It was a slow death," an observer said. "Mt. Gox was like a leaking ship, with the captain constantly bailing water with buckets, but the water kept coming in. Eventually, the ship would sink — it was just a matter of time."

The ship finally sank in 2014. Mt. Gox filed for bankruptcy, admitting to the loss of 850,000 Bitcoin, worth approximately $450 million at the time. Karpeles was arrested and charged with embezzlement and fraud. Mt. Gox became the greatest disaster in Bitcoin history, an eternal warning about greed, incompetence, and the dangers of misplaced trust.

But in 2011, all of this was still in the future. Mt. Gox continued operating, Bitcoin continued thriving, and people continued believing. The omens were ignored, the warnings were forgotten, and the feast continued. Until the final collapse, people would recall that phone call in the early morning of June 2011, that instantaneous price crash, that first dark arrow of fate.

### V. Reflection: The Absurdity of Value and the Inferno of Speculation

Laszlo Hanyecz's 10,000 Bitcoin pizza, Mark Karpeles's Mt. Gox empire, the 2011 price surge and hacking crisis — these stories together constitute the chronicle of the crypto world's early days. What do they tell us?

First, value is absurd. In 2010, 10,000 Bitcoin were worth two pizzas. In 2017, they were worth $200 million. The pizza was the same pizza, Bitcoin was the same Bitcoin, but the value changed by tens of millions of times. This reminds us that market prices do not always reflect true value — value is often merely the product of collective hallucination.

"Value is like flowers in a mirror or water in the moon's reflection," a poet wrote. "It exists, and yet does not. It is real, and yet illusory. We see in it what we want to see — wealth, hope, fear, greed. It is the projection of human psychology, the crystallization of social consensus."

Second, speculation is dangerous. The 2011 price surge attracted countless people into Bitcoin, and most of them were wiped out. Those who bought at high prices suffered heavily in the crash. Those who borrowed money to invest fell into debt crises. Those driven by FOMO became the last bag holders.

"Speculation is the fire of hell," a philosopher warned. "It burns everything — reason, caution, morality. It promises wealth but brings destruction. It drives people mad, blinds them, and makes them slaves to their own greed."

But most importantly, these stories remind us that innovation always comes with risk. Bitcoin was a revolutionary technology, but it also brought new dangers — hacking attacks, exchange collapses, price volatility. We cannot abandon innovation because of risk, but neither can we ignore risk in blind pursuit of returns.

"The crypto world is a marketplace," an old user summarized. "There are heroes, there are villains, there are fortunes made and fortunes lost. It is an amplifier of human nature, a battlefield of greed and idealism. We see ourselves in it — our hopes, our fears, our strengths, our weaknesses."

Laszlo Hanyecz spent his Bitcoin on pizza and became part of history. Mark Karpeles built an empire and watched it collapse. Countless speculators flooded into the market; some got rich, some went broke. This is the turbulence of the crypto world — an endless story, an eternal cycle.

"The storm never ceases," the old man in the preface had said. "Only time will tell who laughs last."

In the summer of 2011, the storm was just beginning to show, the feast was still going on, and the first arrow had already been loosed. The next chapter will be the rise of the mining revolution, the legend of烤猫's rise, and another story about genius and disappearance.

But that is another story for another time. For now, let us remember Laszlo Hanyecz, remember his pizza, remember his idealism. Behind hundreds of millions of dollars' worth of Bitcoin is the aroma of two pizzas, the dream of a programmer, the most expensive dinner in crypto history.

"I don't regret it," Laszlo said many years later. "I did what I believed was right. History will judge me, but I can face that judgment with a clear conscience. I participated in history, I changed the world — even if only in a small way. That's worth more than any wealth."

This is the price of pizza.
